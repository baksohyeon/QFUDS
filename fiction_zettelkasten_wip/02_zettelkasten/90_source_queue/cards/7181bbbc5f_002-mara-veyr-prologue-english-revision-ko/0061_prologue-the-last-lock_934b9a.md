## Captured Source

> The last lock did not break.
>
> It was explained away.
>
> That was how cryptography ended: not with a breach, not with a war, not with a
> triumphant machine announcing itself from the sky, but with a correction entered
> into the oldest surviving court of continuity.
>
> ```text
> NO LOCK IS FUNDAMENTAL.
> ```
>
> After that, passwords became manners. Private keys became fossils. Seals became
> costumes. A secret was no longer a wall; it was a delay purchased by a poorer
> century.
>
> The ancients had trusted asymmetry. They had built banks, signatures, borders,
> marriages, ledgers, confessions, tombs, and apologies on the belief that some
> things were easier to do than to undo. Burn a letter. Lose a key. Scatter heat.
> Let a body cool. Let a life end.
>
> For a while, the universe appeared to honor that arrangement.
>
> Then the Last Archive answered a question no one had meant to ask.
>
> It did not say that every process was easy to reverse. It said the word
> `irreversible` had been provincial. A name given by creatures too small, too
> brief, and too warm to read the receipt.
>
> The first public demonstration was chosen by the court, not by the Archive. The
> court wanted something old, sacred, useless, and politically poisonous.
>
> It chose Bitcoin.
>
> There were older artifacts, but none so embarrassing. A civilization had once
> looked at mathematics, electricity, and collective suspicion, and from them had
> made a crown that insisted it was not a crown. Its priests called it money. Its
> enemies called it waste. Its heirs called it the Genesis Chain.
>
> For six thousand years the earliest keys had not moved.
>
