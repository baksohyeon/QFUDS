## Captured Source

> The Waiting City did not riot all at once.
>
> It learned the rhythm from older institutions: first a queue failed, then a
> rumor found a screen, then a screen borrowed a prayer, then a prayer acquired
> legal vocabulary, and by noon people who had come to retrieve fathers, sons,
> wives, debtors, witnesses, and saints were chanting at a courthouse wall as if
> the wall had invented death.
>
> Liora Sen watched from the east gallery with a paper cup cooling in her hand.
> The tea had gone bitter. That was the only honest thing in the room.
>
> Below, the public square had split around the old market arch. The Ledger
> faithful stood under black banners printed with the first address of the
> Genesis Chain. They had polished the address into a relic until almost no one
> remembered it had once been a tool for moving value between frightened people
> with machines too hot for their apartments.
>
> Across the fountain, the Oblivion Cloisters held blank sheets over their
> heads. No slogans. No faces. No claims. Only paper, refusing to become record.
>
> Between the two groups, ordinary families tried to reach the intake doors.
> They carried wet shoes, food tins, sleeping children, sealed urns, unpaid
> invoices, and the dreadful patience of people who had learned that grief could
> be rescheduled by docket number.
>
> The square shouted at them anyway.
>
> ```text
> NO MASTER OF THE LEDGER.
> NO DEATH OF THE RIGHT.
> ```
>
> The blank papers answered with silence.
>
> "The chant is shorter today," Ione said.
>
> Liora did not turn. "Progress."
>
> "No. Discipline."
>
