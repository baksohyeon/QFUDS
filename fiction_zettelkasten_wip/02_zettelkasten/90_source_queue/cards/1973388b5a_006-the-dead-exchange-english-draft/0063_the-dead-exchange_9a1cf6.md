## Captured Source

> The Dead Exchange opened three hours after the court closed.
>
> It had no bell, no market board, no shouting clerks, and no prices.
>
> That was what the tour guides said, because tour guides were paid to preserve
> the dignity of lies. Liora Sen knew better. The Dead Exchange had prices. It
> had merely outlived the vulgar kind.
>
> No one bought coins there. No one sold coins. No screen showed a rising number
> or a falling one. The last public price of the Genesis Chain had been embalmed
> in schoolbooks, argued over by small sects, and then made useless by the only
> market event that mattered.
>
> The keys had returned.
>
> After that, price became an embarrassment. The old believers had asked whether
> the number would ever reach zero. The century answered by making the question
> provincial. A crown did not need to buy bread. A crown needed only to be
> recognized by people willing to kill, marry, sue, inherit, defect, or pray
> under it.
>
> The Dead Exchange traded recognition.
>
> It lived beneath the eastern vaults of the Court of Continuance, below the
> public galleries, below the intake halls, below the waiting rooms where families
> learned to speak of the dead in admissible grammar. The public entrance was a
> museum arch. The real entrance was a freight lift behind Chamber Two, opened
> only by court salt, Laur sleeve-thread, and the kind of exhausted authority that
> looked like permission to machines.
>
> Liora stood before the lift with the new docket still tacky in her case.
>
> ```text
> MARAE VEYR
> RECOVERABLE / NOT CLAIMABLE
> TEMPORARY AUDITOR MARK
> ```
>
> Temporary, the clerk had insisted.
>
