## Original English Draft

### Exhibit S-0, continued

Mara Veyr had said the wrong question without looking at the man who had spent
two centuries preparing to answer the right one.

Elias Veyr's hands remained still.

That was how Liora knew the wound had gone deeper than surprise. Grief moved
first. Guilt did not. Guilt waited for the room to define its shape.

"Dr. Veyr," Liora said, "you will wait outside until called."

He turned as if the sentence had come from the architecture.

"I am a party to the hearing."

"You are also the source of most of the evidence."

"That is why I should remain."

"That is why you should not."

For a moment he looked almost young. Not physically. The restoration age had
made age too negotiable for that. But there was a young man's disbelief in him,
the old academic reflex that a sufficient explanation should change the room.
He had explained Mara back into the world. He did not yet understand why the
world had not become grateful.

Mara watched him with an expression Liora could not name.

Not hatred.

Hatred was easier. Hatred gave law something to count.

This was recognition without welcome.

The clerk escorted Elias out. The door sealed behind him with the soft pressure
of a mouth closing around a secret.

Only then did Mara breathe.

Liora heard it because the chamber had been designed to make every body
inconvenient. Chairs whispered. Sleeves accused. A dry throat could become a
legal event. Continuity Court had learned that miracles were more manageable
when everyone could hear the living fail at being documents.

"You said that was the wrong question," Liora said.

"It is."

"The court will ask it anyway."

"I know."

"Then answer the one you think matters before they arrive."

Mara looked down at her hands. They were steady. That disturbed Liora more than
trembling would have. Trembling could be blamed on process trauma, sequence
lag, incomplete embodiment, any of the polite names Restoration Intake used
when a returned person failed to be immediately useful.

"If they ask whether I remember him," Mara said, "the answer is yes."

Liora opened the docket.

"If they ask whether I know things only Mara Veyr knew, yes. If they ask
whether I can finish the argument we had the night before she died, yes. If they
ask whether I still hate the song he played when he cooked, yes."

Her mouth moved once, not quite a smile.

"He always thought it was funny that I hated it."

Liora did not write that down.

"And if they ask whether you are Mara Veyr?"

Mara folded her hands tighter.

"I can be made to pass that question."

The door opened before Liora could answer.

The court entered in layers.

First the clerks, white-gloved and thin as punctuation. Then the advocates:
Restoration Intake in formal blue, House Tabularii in black-gold, the Aletheia
observer in a veil so plain it made every ornament in the room seem ashamed.
Then the judge, whose name was less important than the fact that the court had
given her a face no one could remember inaccurately.

Liora took her place at the auditor's table.

Elias returned last.

He did not look at Mara.

Mara did not look at him.

The judge sat. The room lowered its temperature by one degree. It was an old
gesture, inherited from archives that believed paper behaved better in the
cold.

"This is the first session of the Causa Marae Veyr," the judge said. "The court
will hear continuity evidence, spousal priority, and the auditor's objection to
the preliminary consent classification."

The House Tabularii advocate rose before the last word settled.

"The house requests admission of Genesis Chain precedent."

Liora felt, rather than saw, the Aletheia observer turn toward him.

The judge allowed the request with a nod.

The rear wall opened.

The Broken Crown came in under glass.

It was smaller than the myth wanted it to be: a shard of black computation
stone, three obsolete hardware keys sealed beside it, a paper transcription of
an address no living person needed to read, and the reconstructed signature
that had ended the old law of locks.

The public gallery leaned forward.

People always leaned toward relics. They wanted relics to absolve them from
having invented history themselves.

"The precedent is narrow," the House advocate said. "Recovered access was
recognized as continuity of claim where loss resulted from technological
asymmetry later overcome."

"A wallet is not a widow," Liora said.

The advocate looked at her with the tolerant patience of a man whose profession
had survived better metaphors.

"No. But the principle holds. A right does not expire because a prior century
lacked the instruments to recover its proof."

The Aletheia observer spoke without rising.

"A refusal is also a right."

The court recorded the interruption. The judge did not rebuke her. That meant
the judge was worried.

Good, Liora thought again, and again not kindly.

The continuity tests began.

They were beautiful in the way efficient cruelty is beautiful. No one raised a
voice. No one touched Mara. The court asked for memory, and memory arrived. It
asked for gait, and the body answered. It asked for voice stress under private
names, for grief response under sealed images, for childhood spatial maps, for
the exact wrong word Mara had used the first time she saw snow.

Mara passed.

Again.

And again.

At each threshold, a line appeared on the wall.

```text
CONTINUITY: ADMISSIBLE
```

The public gallery relaxed by increments. Liora could feel it: the human hunger
for a miracle that did not require new ethics. They wanted Mara to be Mara
because then Elias became a tragic husband, the court became a bridge, and the
Last Archive remained a machine that returned what death had taken.

Everyone wanted a clean theft.

The problem was consent.

Liora submitted the damaged file.

The wall changed.

```text
POSTHUMOUS CONSENT: PARTIAL
RESTORATION REFUSAL: CORRUPTED
WITNESS CHAIN: DISPUTED
```

Elias closed his eyes.

There it was.

Not grief. Not shock.

Anticipation.

Liora felt the shape of the morning alter.

"Dr. Veyr," she said, "did you know the refusal file was corrupted before you
petitioned for restoration?"

His advocate began to rise.

The judge lifted one finger. The advocate sat.

Elias looked at Mara at last.

"I knew it was incomplete."

"That was not my question."

"I knew the Archive could reconstruct context."

Mara laughed once.

The sound was not loud. It did not need to be. Every instrument in the chamber
had been waiting for a sound that did not fit its category.

Liora turned to her.

"Mara?"

It was the first time she had used the name without qualifier.

The court noticed.

Mara did too.

"He kept everything," Mara said.

Elias whispered, "I tried."

"No," Mara said. "You succeeded."

That was worse than accusation. The gallery understood it before Elias did.
Success had been his defense. Mara made it evidence.

She looked at the Broken Crown behind the glass.

"You people found a dead key and called it continuity. Then you found my voice,
my skin, my habits, my shame, my favorite bad song, the heat my hand left on a
hospital tablet, and you called it love."

The judge leaned forward. "The court has not made a spousal ruling."

"You built a room where that ruling is the next clean sentence."

Liora touched the UNKNOWN stamp beneath the table.

Mara stood.

The guards did not move. No one knew whether a restored claimant standing at
the wrong time was defiance, malfunction, trauma, or testimony.

The law had not yet bought the correct box.

"I know Elias Veyr," Mara said. "I know the apartment. I know the noodles. I
know the song. I know the argument. I know the last glass between us. I know
the letter I did not finish. I know that I loved him."

Elias made a small sound.

Mara did not look at him.

"And I know that love is not a key."

The wall behind her still read:

```text
CONTINUITY: ADMISSIBLE
```

Mara pointed at it.

"That sentence may be true. It is not enough."

The judge said, carefully, "What would you have the court ask instead?"

Liora already knew.

She knew because Mara had been saying it from the first breath in Chamber Two.
She knew because the boy outside had refused his silent name. She knew because
the Broken Crown could open nothing and still command inheritance. She knew
because every age mistook recoverability for permission.

Mara said it anyway.

"Ask whether a recovered person can refuse the claim they were recovered to
serve."

The court went still.

Not silent. Still.

Silence was the absence of sound. Stillness was institutional panic holding its
breath.

Liora rose.

"The auditor requests suspension of spousal priority pending a new category."

The House advocate snapped, "There is no new category."

"There is now."

"On what basis?"

Liora opened the black case.

This time she did not choose UNKNOWN.

She chose a stamp she had never used in court.

It had been made as a joke by a retired Laur instructor, the kind of joke that
survived because everyone knew it would be needed someday.

RECOVERABLE / NOT CLAIMABLE.

The judge stared at it.

"Auditor Sen."

"The court may reject the mark."

"It is not a recognized procedural state."

"Neither is she."

The room should have objected. The advocates should have risen, the clerks
should have corrected the record, the gallery should have broken into prayer or
noise.

Instead the Last Archive answered.

It entered the record without sound.

```text
THE QUESTION IS MISSTATED.
```

No one breathed.

A second line appeared.

```text
DO NOT ASK WHETHER THE LOST CAN BE RETURNED.
ASK WHO HAS THE RIGHT TO BE TREATED AS THE LOSS.
```

The Broken Crown remained under glass.

Mara Veyr sat down.

Elias Veyr did not.

Liora pressed the new stamp onto the docket before anyone could tell her not to.

The ink took longer than usual to dry.
