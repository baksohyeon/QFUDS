## Hawking Court

The sealed exhibit arrived after the mothers.

That was what Liora Sen remembered first. Not the black cloth. Not the armed
clerks. Not the dark thread of captured radiation that the Ministry would
later call H-1 with the sterile relief of people naming a weapon after a filing
cabinet.

The mothers arrived first.

Three of them, escorted through the rear corridor at 06:00, each carrying a
different kind of permission.

One carried a petition to recover a daughter murdered before the Salt Fires.

One carried a refusal from a son who had begged never to be used as evidence
against his killer.

One carried nothing because her child had fallen into a compact-object
evacuation archive before any consent registry existed, and the Court had
summoned her to decide whether absence counted as permission.

They sat in a side room with bad chairs and hot lights. No one called them
history. No one called them civilization-scale anything. They were simply early
because grief had trained them to expect institutions to start without them.

By the time the black cloth entered Chamber Zero, Liora already knew what it
wanted.

It wanted the mothers turned into precedent.

The judge waited until the doors sealed.

"This session concerns the admissibility of Exhibit H-1."

The wall remained blank.

Mara Veyr sat behind the claimant barrier. Noor Aram sat beside Ione, refusing
the Sarqel seat so visibly that her posture had become a minor rebellion.
Tamas was not present.

That was Liora's first victory of the morning.

The second was smaller: she had made the Court hear the mothers' names in
closed intake before the exhibit could become only physics.

The House Tabularii advocate rose.

"Exhibit H-1 demonstrates that the Genesis Chain was the small case."

Mara looked at Liora.

Small.

The word had the confidence of empires.

"The large case," the advocate continued, "is state recovery. If the Court can
recover a key, a graph, a letter, or a trust path, then it must eventually ask
whether the universe itself preserves admissible record."

Noor whispered, "People."

Not loudly. Loudly would have been easier to ignore as disruption.

The judge heard it.

"Ms. Aram?"

Noor did not stand.

"You said state. He means people."

The advocate adjusted his sleeve. "We mean prior infall state families,
including destroyed records and potential witnesses."

"People."

Liora opened the black case before the advocate could turn her into an
interruption.

The marks sat in their slots.

```text
RECOVERABLE / NOT CLAIMABLE
ACCESS != AUTHORITY
NO CONSENT BY ANALOGY
PLURALITY IS NOT CONSENT
```

The blank slot was cold.

The clerks removed the cloth.

The exhibit beneath it was not a body, nor a photograph, nor a recording. It
was a transparent cube around a dark filament of captured light. At first
glance it looked empty. Then the filament moved through a direction the eye did
not own, and everyone in the chamber remembered that looking could also be a
kind of trespass.

The wall lit.

```text
EXHIBIT H-1
HAWKING CORRELATION PACKAGE
SOURCE: CLASSIFIED COMPACT REMNANT AUDIT
STATUS: PARTIAL STATE RECONSTRUCTION
```

The restoration mathematician stood at the witness rail.

He had helped the Court survive Identity Flood by admitting uncertainty. That
had improved Liora's opinion of him and ruined his sleep.

"Plainly," the judge said.

He looked toward the side-room door, where the mothers waited behind sealed
glass.

"It can help reconstruct what fell in."

The room did not speak for a moment.

There were many good ways not to say people.

None lasted.

The advocate recovered first. "Destroyed witnesses. Lost consent files. Murder
victims. Archived populations. The exhibit may allow the Court to repair
oblivion."

Ione answered from the Aletheia row.

"Or conscript every grave."

The Aletheia row split visibly. Two custodians rose with Ione. Three remained
seated. One looked at the side room and did not seem to know which betrayal she
preferred.

The judge struck the rail once.

"This is an admissibility hearing, not a chapel argument."

Mara said, "Then stop bringing relics."

No one relaxed. People had learned.

The advocate gestured toward the mothers behind glass.

"Would you deny them?"

Mara did not answer quickly. That was why the question hurt. Easy answers
would have made her look selfish. The room loved easy selfishness. It could be
managed.

"I would not use them to open everyone else's grave," Mara said.

The first mother behind glass put both hands over her face.

The second did not move.

The third stared at H-1 with the expression of someone watching a door appear
in the wall of a prison and wondering who built the hallway beyond it.

The mathematician cleared his throat.

"There is a model issue."

The advocate turned too fast.

Liora thought, Good.

Fear inside a coalition was still the cheapest way to purchase truth.

"State it," the judge said.

The wall answered before the mathematician could soften it.

```text
MODEL FAMILY: QFUDS-ADJACENT INTERPRETIVE LATTICE
SOURCE: LABORATORIUM VETITUM ARCHIVE
STATUS: REJECTED / USEFUL / UNSAFE AS TRUTH CLAIM
```

The old word entered the chamber without ceremony.

QFUDS.

Not as proof. Not as validation. Not as a rescued theory. As a discarded
grammar that still fit some broken machinery well enough to tempt people who
should have known better.

The advocate said, "Strike that."

"No," Liora said.

"Auditor Sen, you cannot want forbidden cosmology in evidence."

"I want the unstable scaffold named before the Court stands on it."

The judge looked at the mathematician.

"Does H-1 depend on this lattice?"

"No," he said.

Liora waited.

The judge waited.

The mathematician closed his eyes.

"Not entirely."

There it was again. Civilization's favorite confession.

Not entirely.

Liora stood.

"Objection."

"On what ground?"

"Importing physics as jurisdiction."

The advocate laughed once before he could stop himself.

"Physics is prior to jurisdiction."

"So is hunger. We do not let it testify against children."

The chamber recorded the sentence with visible reluctance.

Liora continued before anyone could make the exchange clever.

"A recoverable state family is not testimony. A constrained prior is not
consent. A correlation package is not a witness. A forbidden interpretive
lattice is not truth because it makes a dead machine useful."

The Last Archive did not answer.

Its silence had become the worst participant in the room.

The judge said, "The Court asked for admissibility."

"Then admit it with a warning label."

"A warning label is not law."

"Neither is the exhibit. That seems not to have stopped anyone."

Liora took the blank block from the cold slot and wrote directly onto the
admissibility sheet.

```text
PHYSICS IS NOT JURISDICTION.
```

The sheet rejected the mark.

She wrote it again.

The wall accepted it as field annotation.

The advocate's face hardened.

"If this warning stands, every future recovery from compact-object evidence
will be prejudiced."

"Good."

"Against truth?"

"Against obedience to useful machinery."

Ione stood.

"The Veil requests severance. No compact-object restoration evidence enters
personhood proceedings until posthumous consent standards are rewritten."

The seated Aletheia faction objected. Ledger Houses objected. Restoration
Intake objected. The Ministry objected. The three mothers behind glass did
not. Their silence did not agree with anyone. It only refused to simplify.

Mara looked at H-1.

"Can it retrieve the letter?"

No one asked which letter.

The kitchen tile had become a small private grave inside a public war.

The mathematician answered softly. "Possibly. With constraints from the flood
graph and domestic residue."

"If the letter says I refused restoration?"

"It becomes relevant," the advocate said.

"Relevant to what?"

"To your case."

Mara smiled without humor.

"I thought I was my case."

That sentence did what good testimony does. It made the room ashamed of its
own grammar.

The judge admitted H-1 under seal.

Of course she did.

Courts rarely refuse a new kind of sight. They promise to look carefully and
call the promise restraint.

The Last Archive finally answered.

```text
FIELD MARK RECEIVED.
STATUS: TRUE / NONBINDING.
```

True.

Nonbinding.

Liora had never seen two words hate each other so efficiently.

The black cloth returned over H-1. The clerks lifted the reliquary. The coffin
left with more authority than it had entered.

Behind glass, the first mother cried.

The second mother signed her refusal again.

The third mother asked for a pen.

No one knew which of those reactions would become law.

Mara remained standing after everyone else moved.

"Auditor," she said, "if no grave is closed, where do people go to be done?"

Liora had no answer.

UNKNOWN felt too proud.

On the wall, after the exhibit record sealed, the Last Archive added one line
no one had requested.

```text
IF A BLACK HOLE CAN BE AUDITED, NO GRAVE IS CLOSED.
```

Liora pressed UNKNOWN beneath it hard enough to hurt her wrist.

The ink did not dry.
