## Original English Draft

### Exhibit S-0

The boy in the third queue had three names and was trying not to cry.

Liora Sen noticed him because he was doing it badly. Children who came to the
Waiting City learned early how grief was supposed to look: quiet, folded,
administrative. The boy had not learned. His face kept betraying him in small
illegal motions. A jaw tightened. A breath caught. His hands opened and closed
around the paper bracelet that marked him as a dependent claimant.

The bracelet was blue.

Low-resolution grief insurance.

His mother knelt beside him and whispered the names again.

"Public name."

"Tamas."

"Civic name."

"Tamas Elian Daur."

"Silent name."

The boy looked past her, toward the white towers of the Court of Continuance.
They rose from the morning fog like instruments left standing after an
operation. Around them the Waiting City was already awake: memorial vendors
rolling carts of folded veils, petition clerks calling family numbers, monks
from the Oblivion Cloisters offering blank paper to anyone willing to sign
before hope made them foolish.

"Silent name," his mother repeated.

The boy shook his head.

Good, Liora thought.

She did not mean it kindly. Kindness was too large for the hour. She meant only
that the child still understood one of the few lessons the century had not
managed to ruin: a person should keep at least one door without a handle.

The mother saw Liora watching and lowered her eyes. People recognized Laur
auditors by the narrow grey strip sewn into the left sleeve, by the absence of
family badges, by the small black case carried against the ribs. They did not
know what was in the case. Most assumed it held instruments.

It held stamps.

DEFINED.

ASSUMED.

UNKNOWN.

CIRCULAR IF FITTED.

The old jokes about Laur auditors said they could deny a miracle in four inks.
Liora had never corrected the joke. It was useful for frightened families to
think she hated miracles. Hatred sounded emotional. It made her legible.

The truth was worse. Liora respected miracles. That was why she did not trust
them with paperwork.

At the eastern gate, a grief-broker stepped into her path.

"Auditor Sen. Mara Veyr hearing?"

He was too polished for a broker and too hungry for a clerk. His suit had the
smooth dark finish of the Ledger Houses, but his shoes were city-made and
slightly wet at the seams. Someone had paid him to look inevitable and not
enough to keep him dry.

Liora did not stop.

"You are blocking a court officer."

"I am offering context."

"That is usually worse."

He matched her pace. "The Veyr case will settle before noon. Continuity
probability is above the admissible threshold. Spousal priority is uncontested
except by procedure. No one benefits from delay."

Liora looked at the gate ahead, not at him.

"Everyone benefits from delay. That is why law invented it."

The broker smiled as if she had made a charming old-world remark. "House
Tabularii will submit a Genesis Chain precedent. Recovered access has been
recognized as continuity in three succession courts."

There it was. The dead chain again.

In the oldest classrooms, Bitcoin was taught as a failed currency. In the
newest courts, it was taught as a relic that had survived its own failure. Once
there had been markets, miners, keys, jokes, fortunes, frauds, manifestos, and
men who promised the number would never fall to zero. Time had been unkind to
all of them. The markets died first. The miners became museums. The keys became
fossils.

The manifestos lasted longest.

That was the embarrassment of the Genesis Chain. It had been wrong about money
and right about memory.

"A key is not a spouse," Liora said.

"No," the broker said. "A key is cleaner."

She stopped then.

The city moved around them. Families flowed toward petition windows. A procession
from the Aletheia Veil passed in silence, each woman carrying an empty frame at
her chest. The frames were not symbols. They were legal instruments, gaps
declared in public. Nothing could be restored into them without later review.

The broker seemed pleased to have made her stop.

Liora took the black case from her ribs and opened it.

He glanced down.

"That is not necessary."

"You offered context."

She selected the smallest stamp.

UNKNOWN.

The broker's smile thinned. "You cannot mark a house submission before it is
entered."

"I am not marking the submission."

Liora pressed the stamp onto the broker's calling card. The ink dried without
shine.

"I am marking you."

The eastern gate opened before he could decide whether to be offended. The gate
recognized her sleeve, her pulse, the court salt under her thumbnail, and the
absence of any living family claim against her name. It did not recognize her
judgment. Machines rarely did. They preferred cleaner things: identity,
permission, continuity, thresholds.

Inside the court, a clerk was already waiting.

"Auditor Sen. The Veyr file is ready."

"No file is ready."

The clerk ignored this because clerks survived by ignoring philosophy before
breakfast. She handed Liora a translucent docket sheet. At the top, in careful
legal capitals, the court had written what it wanted the morning to be.

```text
MARAE VEYR: CONTINUITY HEARING
STATUS: ADMISSIBLE
EXPECTED RULING WINDOW: 11:40-12:05
```

Under it, in smaller type, was the line that made the expected window absurd.

```text
POSTHUMOUS CONSENT FILE: DAMAGED
```

Liora held the sheet to the light.

Damage was an old word. It covered too much. Fire damage. Water damage.
Political damage. Grief damage. The sort of damage a man might create by
preserving every trace of his wife except the trace that told him to stop.

"Who classified the consent file?" Liora asked.

"The preliminary office."

"Which office?"

The clerk looked uncomfortable. "Restoration Intake."

Of course.

Restoration Intake existed to make returns possible. It was not built to honor
refusals. Asking it to classify a damaged consent file was like asking a hunger
to audit a kitchen.

Liora placed the docket sheet in her case, beside the stamps.

"Has Mara Veyr arrived?"

"Dr. Veyr has arrived."

"I asked about Mara."

The clerk lowered her voice. "The restored claimant is in Chamber Two."

The restored claimant.

Not wife. Not widow. Not patient. Not deceased. Not living.

The court had already begun hiding from its own nouns.

Liora walked toward Chamber Two alone.

The hallway walls displayed the morning's educational exhibits for the public
gallery. Most days they were harmless: diagrams of continuity thresholds,
children's histories of the Remote Mourning, polished animations explaining why
a recovered voice was not sufficient to establish personhood.

Today the wall showed Exhibit S-0.

No image. Only captions, annotations, and the fossilized laughter of a century
that had thought bad machines were safe machines.

```text
POPULAR BENCHMARK, EARLY SYNTHETIC VIDEO ERA
ANNOTATION: HUMANS STILL FOUND IT HARMLESS
```

Liora slowed.

The exhibit had always annoyed her. Not because it was unserious. Because it
was too honest. The old video did not matter. The famous face did not matter.
The impossible food did not matter. What mattered was the comfort people had
taken from failure. A hand with too many fingers. A mouth that forgot how eating
worked. A scene that declared itself false by being bad at the lie.

For a while, reality had been protected by incompetence.

Then the incompetence improved.

The final annotation appeared beneath the first.

```text
REALITY BECAME EDITABLE WHILE EVERYONE CONTINUED ARGUING ONLINE.
```

Students loved that line. Liora hated it. It made an apocalypse small enough to
quote.

At the end of the corridor, Chamber Two opened.

Dr. Elias Veyr stood inside with his back to the door.

He looked smaller than his file.

Files made people historical. Rooms made them human again, which was one of the
many reasons courts preferred files. Elias had the careful stillness of a man
who had spent too long arranging himself around a single absence. He wore the
black sleeve of a scholar-defendant, no jewelry, no visible interface, no
ornament except the pale crescent burn around his right wrist.

Event-horizon exposure, the file had said.

Unconfirmed.

Liora looked away from the burn before he could see her seeing it.

The woman across from him sat at the witness table with both hands folded in
front of her. Thirty-four years old in body. Centuries older in paperwork.

Mara Veyr looked exactly like the image attached to the file.

That was the first cruelty.

No family deserved that much accuracy.

Mara turned as Liora entered. Her gaze moved first to the door, then to Liora's
sleeve, then to the black case.

Not to Elias.

That was the second cruelty.

"Auditor Sen," Elias said.

His voice had been famous once. Lectures, inquiries, censures, appeals. The
usual ladder by which a brilliant man climbed from grief to doctrine.

Liora did not answer him first.

She addressed Mara.

"Do you know why you are here?"

Mara looked at the docket sheet in Liora's hand.

"The court wants to decide whether I am continuous with Mara Veyr."

"And are you?"

Elias inhaled.

Mara did not.

"That is the wrong question," she said.

Liora opened the black case and touched the UNKNOWN stamp with one finger.

For the first time that morning, she felt almost ready.
