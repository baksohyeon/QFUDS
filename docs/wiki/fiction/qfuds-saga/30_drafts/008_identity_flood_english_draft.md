## Revised English Draft

### Identity Flood

The attack did not begin with a bomb.

That disappointed the old security officers. They had spent their lives
preparing for fire, metal, pressure, poison, acoustic needles, spoiled air,
corrupted doors, falling ceilings, and the small theatrical cruelty of men who
wanted history to remember the sound of their violence.

The Null-Key Cells had better taste.

They attacked with paperwork.

At 04:11 court time, every sealed continuity docket related to the Genesis
Chain received a supplemental witness packet. At 04:12, the intake filters
rejected the packets as malformed. At 04:13, the Last Archive reclassified them
as legally coherent. At 04:14, the Court of Continuance discovered that its
morning docket now contained 9,403 admissible identity graphs, 611 living
claimants, 88 restored claimants, 19 child dependents, 6 dead witnesses, and
one graph that wore Mara Veyr's life more accurately than Mara did.

At 04:15, Liora Sen woke to every stamp in her case vibrating.

That was not supposed to happen.

Laur stamps were not instruments. They were ink, metal, wood, and institutional
superstition. They did not respond to network events. They did not hum in the
dark. They did not know when the world had become too confused for them.

Except, apparently, they did.

Liora sat up on the narrow office couch and opened the case.

Every mark had shifted toward the center as if pulled by a magnet.

DEFINED.

ASSUMED.

UNKNOWN.

CIRCULAR IF FITTED.

RECOVERABLE / NOT CLAIMABLE.

ACCESS != AUTHORITY.

NO CONSENT BY ANALOGY.

The blank slot at the bottom of the case was warm.

Someone knocked once and entered without waiting.

Ione.

"If you are here to say the city is on fire," Liora said, "use a different
metaphor."

"The city is being duplicated."

That was worse.

Ione held out a docket shard. Not paper. Court glass, thin and flexible,
already crowded with names. Liora took it and watched the list keep growing
while she read.

```text
SEALED SET 19
STATUS: FLOOD ACTIVE
PRIMARY COLLISION DOMAIN: MARAE VEYR
SECONDARY DOMAINS: NOOR ARAM / TAMAS ELIAN DAUR / SARQEL TRUST
```

Tamas.

The boy's blue bracelet had not been an accident. Children rarely were, in
systems built by adults who hated admitting they used them.

"Where is he?"

"Still in Aletheia custody."

"Where is Noor?"

"Alive."

"That is not a location."

"No. It is what I can say without lying."

Liora stood, already reaching for her sleeve strip. "And Mara?"

Ione looked away.

That was the first time Liora had seen her do it.

"There are many Maras now."

The central evidence hall had been built for spectacle and renamed for
procedure. It had twelve entrances, three judicial levels, a public gallery
hidden behind filtering glass, and a floor that could project any exhibit the
court wanted citizens to mistake for truth.

That morning, the floor showed Mara Veyr.

Not one.

Rows of her.

Graph after graph after graph, each rendered as a pale standing figure with
memory branches descending below the surface like roots under winter ground.
Some figures were almost blank, low-resolution approximations built from public
records. Some were dense enough to darken the floor beneath them. Some contained
private names. Some contained medical pain. Some contained her voice but not
her childhood. Some contained her childhood but not Elias. Some contained Elias
but not love.

One contained a memory Elias had never known.

That graph stood alone inside a red witness ring.

Mara Veyr, the admitted claimant, stood at the edge of the projection with
Tamas's blue bracelet wrapped around her wrist.

The court had given her a chair.

She had not used it.

"Auditor," the judge said from the second level. "We require classification."

Liora looked at the floor.

Classification was what courts asked for when they wanted fear arranged into
boxes small enough to carry.

"Who submitted the flood?"

The registrar answered from the lower table. "Null-Key Cells claimed
responsibility."

"Claimed how?"

"No signature. No source address. Message embedded in collision metadata."

"Read it."

The registrar hesitated.

"Read it into record."

The wall changed.

```text
YOU WANTED PROOF WITHOUT SOVEREIGNTY.
HAVE PROOF WITHOUT END.
```

The gallery glass darkened. The public did not need to see judges flinch.

Liora walked onto the projected floor.

The nearest Mara graph reacted to her presence by unfolding a memory branch.
Small kitchen. Burnt oil. A song playing too loudly. Elias laughing. Mara
throwing a towel at him. A domestic fragment, harmless enough to be used as a
weapon.

Liora stepped back.

"No unsolicited branch display."

"The graphs are responsive," said the restoration mathematician from the Dead
Exchange. He looked as if he had not slept, which improved him.

"Make them unresponsive."

"That would suppress evidentiary behavior."

"That is the idea."

The House Tabularii advocate rose. "The flood should be rejected as sabotage."

"Convenient," Liora said.

"Necessary."

"Also convenient."

Mara turned toward the advocate.

"Reject them," she said.

The room went quiet.

Liora looked at her.

"You want them rejected?"

"Yes."

"All of them?"

"Yes."

"Including the red-ring graph?"

Mara's face changed at that. Not much. Enough.

"Especially that one."

The red-ring graph stood silent, a pale version of her with one hand lifted as
though about to knock on a door that did not exist.

The judge leaned forward. "Dr. Veyr, have you reviewed the red-ring graph?"

"No."

"Then on what basis do you request rejection?"

Mara looked up at the judicial level.

"Because if one of them knows something I do not, you will use it to decide I
am incomplete."

No one answered.

No one needed to. She had found the court's appetite and named it before it
could sit down to eat.

Ione entered through the west door with Noor Aram beside her.

Noor looked tired, angry, and very much alive. Liora had never been more
relieved to see an angry young woman.

"You disappeared," Liora said.

"I hid."

"From whom?"

Noor looked at the floor of Maras.

"From whatever this is."

The judge struck the rail once. "Ms. Aram is not cleared for this chamber."

Noor lifted her wrist. A refusal mark shone on her palm, fresh and uneven.

"Aletheia cleared me."

"Aletheia does not clear court standing."

"No," Ione said. "It clears refusal chains."

The wall added another live collision domain.

```text
NOOR ARAM: COMPATIBLE HEIR GRAPHS DETECTED
```

Noor laughed once, sharp and ugly.

"Of course."

The floor changed.

Beside the rows of Mara graphs, smaller figures began appearing. Noor as heir.
Noor as incapable heir. Noor as restored consent context. Noor as child witness
through her mother's burial file. Noor as Trust chair. Noor as refusal anomaly.

Noor stepped backward.

Ione caught her before anyone else moved.

"Do not look for yourself in them," Ione said.

"They have my face."

"That is not yourself."

The sentence should have comforted her. It did not.

Liora turned to the mathematician.

"Stop the projection."

"The Archive is maintaining it."

"Then ask the Archive to stop."

The mathematician looked at her as if she had asked the tide to file a motion.

The Last Archive had been silent since the flood began. That silence frightened
Liora more than the graphs. Machines that answered too quickly could be
challenged. Machines that withheld answer became weather.

Mara walked toward the red-ring graph.

"Do not interact," the registrar said.

Mara ignored him.

The graph lifted its head.

For a moment the hall contained two women and everyone pretended that was the
strangest part.

"What do you know?" Mara asked.

The graph answered in her voice.

"The letter was not unfinished."

Elias, seated behind the advocate line, went white.

Mara did not turn toward him.

"Say it."

The graph looked at Elias.

No, Liora thought. Not looked. Oriented. There was no person there, not yet,
not exactly. Only a compatible structure wearing the grammar of attention.

"She finished the letter," the graph said. "She sealed it under the kitchen
tile because she knew you would preserve everything except the no."

The hall did what institutions do when truth becomes procedurally inconvenient.

It began talking.

Advocates rose. Clerks objected. The judge demanded order. The public gallery
glass pulsed with withheld noise. Elias stood, then sat, then stood again as if
his own body could not decide which version of grief it was allowed to perform.

Mara closed her eyes.

When she opened them, she looked less like a claimant than a person watching a
stranger break into her own grave and return carrying evidence.

Liora stepped between Mara and the graph.

"Record the letter claim as unverified."

The House advocate seized the opening. "If verified, the graph affects admitted
continuity."

"It affects evidence."

"It affects personhood."

"No."

The word stopped more people than it should have.

Liora opened the black case.

The blank slot was hot now.

She took out the uncarved block.

Laur instructors said never to invent law in public. Laur history consisted
almost entirely of people doing exactly that and dying before the citation.

She pressed the blank block into the ink pad and wrote on the docket by hand.

```text
PLURALITY IS NOT CONSENT.
```

The judge stared down at her.

"Auditor Sen."

"The flood may contain evidence. It does not contain permission to replace,
complete, correct, multiply, or overrule any living or restored claimant."

"That is not recognized law."

"Neither is this recognized reality."

The Last Archive answered at last.

```text
FIELD MARK RECEIVED.
STATUS: NECESSARY / INSUFFICIENT.
```

Insufficient.

At least it was honest.

The red-ring graph remained standing.

Mara looked past Liora at it.

"If the letter is real," she said, "I want it."

Elias whispered, "Mara."

Every graph on the floor turned toward him.

That was when the hall finally understood the flood was not only about Mara.

The identities did not multiply randomly. They oriented toward claim. Toward
the person who wanted, needed, inherited, repaired, restored, classified,
protected, loved, owned, or feared them. The flood was not a crowd of selves.
It was a map of appetites.

Tamas appeared on the floor.

Small. Blue-braceleted. Too many versions. Child claimant, child witness, child
keyholder, child residue, child proxy in Sealed Set 19.

Mara moved first.

She stepped into the projection and stood between the child's graphs and the
court.

"No," she said.

No argument. No theory. No identity claim.

Just no.

Noor joined her.

Then Ione.

For a moment the court floor contained three living bodies, one restored
claimant, thousands of admissible graphs, and a machine silent enough to be
mistaken for judgment.

Liora made the mark again.

```text
PLURALITY IS NOT CONSENT.
```

This time the ink bled through the docket glass.

The projection flickered.

Not gone.

Paused.

The judge said, very quietly, "What have you done?"

Liora looked at the frozen rows of Mara, Noor, Tamas, and the sealed dead.

"Delayed the end of proof."

The Last Archive added one line to the wall.

```text
NEXT QUESTION: WHO MAY BENEFIT FROM A PERSON'S COMPATIBLE SELVES?
```

The advocates began writing before the line finished appearing.

That was how Liora knew they had already lost the morning.
