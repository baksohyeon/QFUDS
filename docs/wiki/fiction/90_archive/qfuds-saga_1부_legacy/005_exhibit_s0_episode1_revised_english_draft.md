## Revised English Draft

### Exhibit S-0

The boy in the third queue had three names and was trying not to cry.

Liora Sen noticed him because he was doing it badly. Children who came to the
Waiting City learned early how grief was supposed to look: quiet, folded,
administrative. The boy had not learned. His face kept betraying him in small
illegal motions. A jaw tightened. A breath caught. His hands opened and closed
around the blue paper bracelet that marked him as a dependent claimant.

Blue meant low-resolution grief insurance.

His mother knelt beside him and whispered the names again.

"Public name."

"Tamas."

"Civic name."

"Tamas Elian Daur."

"Silent name."

The boy looked past her, toward the white towers of the Court of Continuance.
They rose from the morning fog like instruments left standing after an
operation. Around them the Waiting City had already begun its work: memorial
vendors rolling carts of folded veils, petition clerks calling family numbers,
monks from the Oblivion Cloisters offering blank paper to anyone willing to
sign before hope made them foolish.

"Silent name," his mother repeated.

The boy shook his head.

Good, Liora thought.

She did not mean it kindly. Kindness was too large for the hour. She meant only
that the child still understood one of the few lessons the century had not
managed to ruin: a person should keep at least one door without a handle.

The mother saw Liora watching and lowered her eyes. People recognized Laur
auditors by the narrow grey strip sewn into the left sleeve, by the absence of
family badges, by the small black case carried against the ribs. They did not
know what was in the case. Most assumed it held instruments.

It held stamps.

DEFINED.

ASSUMED.

UNKNOWN.

CIRCULAR IF FITTED.

The old jokes said Laur auditors could deny a miracle in four inks. Liora had
never corrected the joke. It was useful for frightened families to think she
hated miracles. Hatred sounded emotional. It made her legible.

The truth was worse. Liora respected miracles. That was why she did not trust
them with paperwork.

At the eastern gate, a grief-broker stepped into her path.

"Auditor Sen. Mara Veyr hearing?"

He was too polished for a broker and too hungry for a clerk. His suit had the
smooth dark finish of the Ledger Houses, but his shoes were city-made and wet
at the seams. Someone had paid him to look inevitable and not enough to keep
him dry.

Liora did not stop.

"You are blocking a court officer."

"I am offering context."

"That is usually worse."

He matched her pace. "The Veyr case will settle before noon. Continuity
probability is above the admissible threshold. Spousal priority is uncontested
except by procedure. No one benefits from delay."

"Everyone benefits from delay. That is why law invented it."

The broker smiled as if she had made a charming old-world remark. "House
Tabularii will submit a Genesis Chain precedent. Recovered access has been
recognized as continuity in three succession courts."

There it was. The dead chain again.

In the oldest classrooms, Bitcoin was taught as a failed currency. In the
newest courts, it was taught as a relic that had survived its own failure. Once
there had been markets, miners, keys, jokes, fortunes, frauds, manifestos, and
men who promised the number would never fall to zero. Time had been unkind to
all of them. The markets died first. The miners became museums. The keys became
fossils.

The manifestos lasted longest.

That was the embarrassment of the Genesis Chain. It had been wrong about money
and right about memory.

"A key is not a spouse," Liora said.

"No," the broker said. "A key is cleaner."

She stopped then.

A procession from the Aletheia Veil passed in silence, each woman carrying an
empty frame at her chest. The frames were not symbols. They were legal
instruments, gaps declared in public. Nothing could be restored into them
without later review.

Liora opened the black case.

"That is not necessary," the broker said.

"You offered context."

She selected the smallest stamp.

UNKNOWN.

The broker's smile thinned. "You cannot mark a house submission before it is
entered."

"I am not marking the submission."

Liora pressed the stamp onto his calling card. The ink dried without shine.

"I am marking you."

The eastern gate opened before he could decide whether to be offended. It
recognized her sleeve, her pulse, the court salt under her thumbnail, and the
absence of any living family claim against her name. It did not recognize her
judgment. Machines rarely did. They preferred cleaner things: identity,
permission, continuity, thresholds.

Inside, a clerk waited with the Veyr docket.

```text
MARAE VEYR: CONTINUITY HEARING
STATUS: ADMISSIBLE
EXPECTED RULING WINDOW: 11:40-12:05
```

Under it, in smaller type, was the line that made the expected window absurd.

```text
POSTHUMOUS CONSENT FILE: DAMAGED
```

"Who classified the consent file?" Liora asked.

"The preliminary office."

"Which office?"

The clerk looked uncomfortable. "Restoration Intake."

Of course.

Restoration Intake existed to make returns possible. It was not built to honor
refusals. Asking it to classify a damaged consent file was like asking a hunger
to audit a kitchen.

"Has Mara Veyr arrived?"

"Dr. Veyr has arrived."

"I asked about Mara."

The clerk lowered her voice. "The restored claimant is in Chamber Two."

The restored claimant.

Not wife. Not widow. Not patient. Not deceased. Not living.

The court had already begun hiding from its own nouns.

Liora walked toward Chamber Two alone.

The hallway walls displayed the morning's educational exhibits for the public
gallery. Most days they were harmless: diagrams of continuity thresholds,
children's histories of the Remote Mourning, polished animations explaining why
a recovered voice was not sufficient to establish personhood.

Today the wall showed Exhibit S-0.

No image. Only captions, annotations, and the fossilized laughter of a century
that had thought bad machines were safe machines.

```text
POPULAR BENCHMARK, EARLY SYNTHETIC VIDEO ERA
ANNOTATION: HUMANS STILL FOUND IT HARMLESS
```

Liora slowed.

The old video did not matter. The famous face did not matter. The impossible
food did not matter. What mattered was the comfort people had taken from
failure. A hand with too many fingers. A mouth that forgot how eating worked.
A scene that declared itself false by being bad at the lie.

For a while, reality had been protected by incompetence.

Then the incompetence improved.

The final annotation appeared beneath the first.

```text
REALITY BECAME EDITABLE WHILE EVERYONE CONTINUED ARGUING ONLINE.
```

Students loved that line. Liora hated it. It made an apocalypse small enough to
quote.

At the end of the corridor, Chamber Two opened.

Dr. Elias Veyr stood inside with his back to the door.

He looked smaller than his file.

Files made people historical. Rooms made them human again, which was one of the
many reasons courts preferred files. Elias had the careful stillness of a man
who had spent too long arranging himself around a single absence. He wore the
black sleeve of a scholar-defendant, no jewelry, no visible interface, no
ornament except the pale crescent burn around his right wrist.

Event-horizon exposure, the file had said.

Unconfirmed.

Liora looked away from the burn before he could see her seeing it.

The woman across from him sat at the witness table with both hands folded in
front of her. Thirty-four years old in body. Centuries older in paperwork.

Mara Veyr looked exactly like the image attached to the file.

That was the first cruelty.

No family deserved that much accuracy.

Mara turned as Liora entered. Her gaze moved first to the door, then to Liora's
sleeve, then to the black case.

Not to Elias.

That was the second cruelty.

"Do you know why you are here?" Liora asked.

Mara looked at the docket sheet.

"The court wants to decide whether I am continuous with Mara Veyr."

"And are you?"

Elias inhaled.

Mara did not.

"That is the wrong question," she said.

Liora opened the black case and touched the UNKNOWN stamp with one finger.

For the first time that morning, she felt almost ready.

She sent Elias out before the hearing began.

He objected, as brilliant men often objected, by believing explanation was a
form of permission.

"I am a party to the hearing."

"You are also the source of most of the evidence," Liora said.

"That is why I should remain."

"That is why you should not."

Mara watched him leave with an expression Liora could not name.

Not hatred.

Hatred was easier. Hatred gave law something to count.

This was recognition without welcome.

Only after the door sealed did Mara breathe.

"If they ask whether I remember him," Mara said, "the answer is yes. If they
ask whether I know things only Mara Veyr knew, yes. If they ask whether I can
finish the argument we had the night before she died, yes. If they ask whether
I still hate the song he played when he cooked, yes."

Her mouth moved once, not quite a smile.

"He always thought it was funny that I hated it."

Liora did not write that down.

"And if they ask whether you are Mara Veyr?"

Mara folded her hands tighter.

"I can be made to pass that question."

The court entered in layers.

First the clerks, white-gloved and thin as punctuation. Then the advocates:
Restoration Intake in formal blue, House Tabularii in black-gold, the Aletheia
observer in a veil so plain it made every ornament in the room seem ashamed.
Then the judge, whose name was less important than the fact that the court had
given her a face no one could remember inaccurately.

Elias returned last.

He did not look at Mara.

Mara did not look at him.

The judge sat. The room lowered its temperature by one degree. It was an old
gesture, inherited from archives that believed paper behaved better in the
cold.

"This is the first session of the Causa Marae Veyr," the judge said. "The court
will hear continuity evidence, spousal priority, and the auditor's objection to
the preliminary consent classification."

The House Tabularii advocate rose before the last word settled.

"The house requests admission of Genesis Chain precedent."

Liora felt, rather than saw, the Aletheia observer turn toward him.

The judge allowed the request with a nod.

The rear wall opened.

The Broken Crown came in under glass.

It was smaller than the myth wanted it to be: a shard of black computation
stone, three obsolete hardware keys sealed beside it, a paper transcription of
an address no living person needed to read, and the reconstructed signature
that had ended the old law of locks.

The public gallery leaned forward.

People always leaned toward relics. They wanted relics to absolve them from
having invented history themselves.

"The precedent is narrow," the House advocate said. "Recovered access was
recognized as continuity of claim where loss resulted from technological
asymmetry later overcome."

"A wallet is not a widow," Liora said.

"No," the advocate said. "But the principle holds. A right does not expire
because a prior century lacked the instruments to recover its proof."

The Aletheia observer spoke without rising.

"A refusal is also a right."

The court recorded the interruption. The judge did not rebuke her. That meant
the judge was worried.

Good, Liora thought again, and again not kindly.

The continuity tests began.

They were beautiful in the way efficient cruelty is beautiful. No one raised a
voice. No one touched Mara. The court asked for memory, and memory arrived. It
asked for gait, and the body answered. It asked for voice stress under private
names, for grief response under sealed images, for childhood spatial maps, for
the exact wrong word Mara had used the first time she saw snow.

Mara passed.

Again.

And again.

At each threshold, a line appeared on the wall.

```text
CONTINUITY: ADMISSIBLE
```

The public gallery relaxed by increments. Liora could feel it: the human hunger
for a miracle that did not require new ethics. They wanted Mara to be Mara
because then Elias became a tragic husband, the court became a bridge, and the
Last Archive remained a machine that returned what death had taken.

Everyone wanted a clean theft.

The problem was consent.

Liora submitted the damaged file.

The wall changed.

```text
POSTHUMOUS CONSENT: PARTIAL
RESTORATION REFUSAL: CORRUPTED
WITNESS CHAIN: DISPUTED
```

Elias closed his eyes.

There it was.

Not grief. Not shock.

Anticipation.

"Dr. Veyr," Liora said, "did you know the refusal file was corrupted before you
petitioned for restoration?"

His advocate began to rise.

The judge lifted one finger. The advocate sat.

Elias looked at Mara at last.

"I knew it was incomplete."

"That was not my question."

"I knew the Archive could reconstruct context."

Mara laughed once.

The sound was not loud. It did not need to be. Every instrument in the chamber
had been waiting for a sound that did not fit its category.

"He kept everything," Mara said.

Elias whispered, "I tried."

"No," Mara said. "You succeeded."

That was worse than accusation. The gallery understood it before Elias did.
Success had been his defense. Mara made it evidence.

She looked at the Broken Crown behind the glass.

"You people found a dead key and called it continuity. Then you found my voice,
my skin, my habits, my shame, my favorite bad song, the heat my hand left on a
hospital tablet, and you called it love."

The judge leaned forward. "The court has not made a spousal ruling."

"You built a room where that ruling is the next clean sentence."

Mara stood.

The guards did not move. No one knew whether a restored claimant standing at
the wrong time was defiance, malfunction, trauma, or testimony.

The law had not yet bought the correct box.

"I know Elias Veyr," Mara said. "I know the apartment. I know the noodles. I
know the song. I know the argument. I know the last glass between us. I know
the letter I did not finish. I know that I loved him."

Elias made a small sound.

Mara did not look at him.

"And I know that love is not a key."

The wall behind her still read:

```text
CONTINUITY: ADMISSIBLE
```

Mara pointed at it.

"That sentence may be true. It is not enough."

The judge said, carefully, "What would you have the court ask instead?"

Liora already knew.

She knew because Mara had been saying it from the first breath in Chamber Two.
She knew because the boy outside had refused his silent name. She knew because
the Broken Crown could open nothing and still command inheritance. She knew
because every age mistook recoverability for permission.

Mara said it anyway.

"Ask whether a recovered person can refuse the claim they were recovered to
serve."

The court went still.

Not silent. Still.

Silence was the absence of sound. Stillness was institutional panic holding its
breath.

Liora rose.

"The auditor requests suspension of spousal priority pending a new category."

The House advocate snapped, "There is no new category."

"There is now."

"On what basis?"

Liora opened the black case.

This time she did not choose UNKNOWN.

She chose a stamp she had never used in court.

It had been made as a joke by a retired Laur instructor, the kind of joke that
survived because everyone knew it would be needed someday.

RECOVERABLE / NOT CLAIMABLE.

The judge stared at it.

"Auditor Sen."

"The court may reject the mark."

"It is not a recognized procedural state."

"Neither is she."

The room should have objected. The advocates should have risen, the clerks
should have corrected the record, the gallery should have broken into prayer or
noise.

Instead the Last Archive answered.

It entered the record without sound.

```text
THE QUESTION IS MISSTATED.
```

No one breathed.

A second line appeared.

```text
DO NOT ASK WHETHER THE LOST CAN BE RETURNED.
ASK WHO HAS THE RIGHT TO BE TREATED AS THE LOSS.
```

The Broken Crown remained under glass.

Mara Veyr sat down.

Elias Veyr did not.

Liora pressed the new stamp onto the docket before anyone could tell her not to.

The ink took longer than usual to dry.
