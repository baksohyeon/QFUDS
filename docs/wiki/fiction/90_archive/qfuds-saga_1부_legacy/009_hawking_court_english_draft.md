compatible selves as evidence, the next temptation is to treat the universe
itself as a witness. This episode makes that temptation visible and dangerous.

## Revised English Draft

### Hawking Court

The sealed exhibit arrived in a coffin.

That was not its legal name. The court record called it a thermodynamic
containment reliquary with limited witness access. The Restoration Ministry
called it a historic radiation correlation package. House Tabularii called it
late evidence. The Aletheia Veil called it a sacrilege before anyone opened the
lid.

Liora Sen called it a coffin because some words still had the decency to be
afraid of themselves.

The reliquary entered Chamber Zero under black cloth and six armed clerks. No
public gallery. No remote feed. No educational captions on the wall. The court
had discovered secrecy again, which meant it expected to do something
educational later and did not want witnesses before the lesson was tidy.

Mara Veyr sat behind the claimant barrier.

Noor Aram sat beside Ione in the Aletheia row, refusing the Trust seat so
visibly that even her posture had become a legal document.

Tamas was not present.

That was Liora's only victory that morning.

The judge waited until the doors sealed.

"This session concerns the admissibility of Exhibit H-1."

The wall remained blank.

No one liked a blank wall in court. Walls were supposed to explain danger into
obedience.

The House Tabularii advocate rose.

"Exhibit H-1 establishes that the Genesis Chain precedent is not an isolated
artifact of obsolete cryptography. It is the small case."

Small, Liora thought.

The word entered the room like a knife wrapped in silk.

"The large case," the advocate continued, "is state recovery."

Ione closed her eyes.

Mara did not.

Liora opened the black case on the table before her. The stamps sat in their
slots like a row of inadequate ancestors.

RECOVERABLE / NOT CLAIMABLE.

ACCESS != AUTHORITY.

NO CONSENT BY ANALOGY.

PLURALITY IS NOT CONSENT.

The blank slot was cold again.

That worried her more than heat.

The judge nodded to the clerks.

The coffin opened.

Inside was not a body, nor an image, nor an old machine. It was a cube of
transparent shielding around a dark thread of captured light. At first glance
it looked empty. Then Liora saw the thread move, not across the cube but through
some private direction the eye did not own.

The wall lit.

```text
EXHIBIT H-1
HAWKING CORRELATION PACKAGE
SOURCE: CLASSIFIED COMPACT REMNANT AUDIT
STATUS: PARTIAL STATE RECONSTRUCTION
```

There it was.

The thing everyone had been avoiding with better nouns.

Black-hole audit.

The restoration mathematician stood at the witness rail. He looked older than
he had during Identity Flood. Either he had finally slept or finally understood
what he had helped build.

"The package contains correlations sufficient to constrain prior infall state
families," he said.

"Plainly," the judge said.

He swallowed.

"It can help reconstruct what fell in."

Noor whispered, "People."

No one corrected her.

The House advocate seized the word before it could become accusation.

"People, property, sealed records, murdered witnesses, destroyed cities. The
Archive has shown that information loss was historically overstated."

Liora rose.

"Objection."

"On what ground?" the judge asked.

"Importing physics as jurisdiction."

The advocate almost smiled. "Physics is not imported. It is prior."

"So is gravity. We do not let it inherit."

The judge's face did not change, which meant she had found the objection less
absurd than she wanted to.

"Clarify."

Liora looked at the dark thread.

"A recoverable state family is not testimony. A constrained prior is not
consent. A correlation package is not a witness with rights waived by
thermodynamics."

The House advocate said, "No one is claiming waiver."

"You are claiming admissibility."

"For evidence."

"Evidence against whom?"

The question did not stop the room. It redirected it.

That was sometimes enough.

The wall changed without request.

```text
RELATED PROCEEDINGS:
MARAE VEYR
SARQEL FRAGMENT
SEALED SET 19
UNNAMED COMPACT-OBJECT RESTORATION PETITIONS: 417
```

Four hundred seventeen.

Liora heard Noor breathe in. Heard Mara not breathe at all.

"Those petitions are not before this court," the judge said.

The Last Archive answered.

```text
NOT YET.
```

The two words did more damage than any manifesto.

The Aletheia row split before anyone officially spoke. Half rose with Ione.
Half stayed seated. Liora had known the Veil disagreed internally. She had not
known the fault line would become visible under court lights.

A woman in the seated half spoke first.

"If H-1 can recover murder victims, destroyed consent files, and erased witness
chains, refusing it is complicity with oblivion."

Ione turned toward her.

"And if H-1 can compel every dead person into evidence?"

"Then we build rules."

"We are in a room because rules failed."

The judge struck the rail. "The court will not become a chapel argument."

"It already has relics," Mara said.

Everyone looked at her.

She had not risen. She did not need to. The room had learned by now that Mara
Veyr standing was dangerous, but Mara Veyr sitting was worse. It meant she had
decided the room should come to her.

"Exhibit S-0," she said. "The Broken Crown. Sarqel Fragment. Now this."

She looked at the dark thread.

"How many dead things must you put under glass before you admit this is a
religion?"

The House advocate said, "Dr. Veyr, with respect, you are alive because this
civilization chose not to treat the dead as irretrievable."

Mara smiled.

No one relaxed.

"I am here because one man loved me in a century that had forgotten how to let
love fail."

Elias was not in the room.

That was another of Liora's temporary victories. The court had agreed he should
not attend Exhibit H-1 admissibility. The agreement had lasted only because
everyone knew his absence made him more useful later.

The mathematician touched the witness rail.

"There is another issue."

The House advocate's head turned too quickly.

Good, Liora thought.

Fear inside a coalition was the only reliable source of truth.

"State it," the judge said.

"The state-family model used to interpret H-1 was not developed under Ministry
protocol."

"Who developed it?"

The room knew before the wall answered.

Forbidden things have gravity.

```text
MODEL FAMILY: QFUDS-ADJACENT INTERPRETIVE LATTICE
SOURCE: LABORATORIUM VETITUM ARCHIVE
STATUS: REJECTED / USEFUL
```

Rejected / useful.

That was the most dangerous classification in science, law, and love.

Liora felt the old QFUDS word land in the room. Not as proof. Not as truth. As
temptation. A beautiful failed grammar that still helped a machine sort the
dark.

"Strike that from the record," the House advocate said.

"No," Liora said.

He turned on her. "You want forbidden theory in evidence?"

"I want every unstable support named before you build a bridge on it."

The judge looked at the mathematician.

"Does H-1 depend on this model family?"

"No."

Liora waited.

The judge also waited.

The mathematician closed his eyes.

"Not entirely."

There it was. The court's oldest prayer.

Not entirely.

Liora selected no stamp.

The available marks were too small.

She took the blank block again and wrote directly onto the H-1 admissibility
sheet.

```text
PHYSICS IS NOT JURISDICTION.
```

The judge said, "Auditor Sen, that is not a ruling."

"No. It is a warning label."

"The court asked for admissibility."

"Then admit the exhibit with the label."

"And if the label prejudices interpretation?"

"Good."

The Last Archive remained silent.

That was worse than objection.

The House advocate stepped closer to the reliquary.

"We are past the old fear. Cryptographic Death taught us that one-way relations
were never sacred. Identity Flood taught us that proof must be managed, not
feared. H-1 teaches us that the universe keeps records deeper than law."

Liora looked at the judge.

"That sentence is why the exhibit is dangerous."

"Because it is wrong?"

"Because it may be useful."

Mara stood.

This time the guards moved.

Not toward her. Away.

People learn.

"If H-1 is admitted," Mara said, "can the court retrieve the letter?"

No one spoke.

The sealed letter. The one the red-ring graph had claimed was hidden under a
kitchen tile. The no Elias had not preserved.

The mathematician said, "Possibly."

"From where?"

"Residual thermal, civic, and domestic traces. The graph gives a constraint."

"And if the letter says I refused restoration?"

The House advocate said, "Then it becomes relevant."

"Relevant to what?"

"To your case."

Mara laughed softly.

"I thought I was my case."

That sentence did what good testimony does. It made the room ashamed of its own
grammar.

Ione stepped forward.

"The Veil requests severance. No compact-object restoration evidence may enter
personhood proceedings until posthumous consent standards are rewritten."

The seated Aletheia faction objected.

The Ledger Houses objected.

Restoration Intake objected.

The Ministry objected.

The judge looked almost relieved. Too many objections could be called balance.

Liora pressed her warning label into the admissibility sheet.

The ink spread across the glass.

```text
PHYSICS IS NOT JURISDICTION.
```

The Last Archive answered at last.

```text
FIELD MARK RECEIVED.
STATUS: TRUE / NONBINDING.
```

Liora stared at the word nonbinding.

That was the future speaking in the voice of a clerk.

The judge admitted H-1 under seal.

Of course she did.

Courts rarely refuse a new kind of sight. They only promise to look carefully.

The reliquary was closed. The black cloth returned. The coffin left Chamber
Zero with more authority than it had entered.

Mara remained standing after everyone else moved.

"Auditor," she said.

Liora turned.

"If no grave is closed," Mara asked, "where do people go to be done?"

Liora did not have an answer.

For once, UNKNOWN felt too proud.

On the wall, after the exhibit record sealed, the Last Archive added one line
no one had requested.

```text
IF A BLACK HOLE CAN BE AUDITED, NO GRAVE IS CLOSED.
```

Liora took out the smallest stamp.

UNKNOWN.

She pressed it beneath the line, hard enough to hurt her wrist.

The ink did not dry.
