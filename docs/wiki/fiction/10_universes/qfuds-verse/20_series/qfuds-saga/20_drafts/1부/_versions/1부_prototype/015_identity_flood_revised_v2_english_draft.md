## Identity Flood

The Null-Key Cells did not send a manifesto.

They sent a receipt.

It arrived on Liora Sen's desk at 04:11 with no sender, no route, no threat,
and no decoration. One line of text. One impossible docket seal.

```text
YOU ASKED THE DEAD FOR CLEAN PROOF.
WE RETURNED THE MESS.
```

At 04:12, every Genesis-related sealed docket received a supplemental witness
packet.

At 04:13, intake rejected the packets as sabotage.

At 04:14, the Last Archive reclassified them as coherent.

At 04:15, Liora woke to every stamp in her black case vibrating against the
floor.

She had slept on the office couch with one boot still on and a dead cup of tea
under the desk. The stamps were not supposed to move. Laur stamps were wood,
metal, ink, and institutional superstition. They did not know when evidence had
become a weather system.

Except they did.

DEFINED had shifted toward UNKNOWN.

UNKNOWN had rolled against `NO CONSENT BY ANALOGY`.

The blank slot at the bottom of the case was warm.

Someone knocked once and entered without waiting.

Ione, carrying two wrist seals and a face that had stopped pretending calm was
a virtue.

"If this is about doors," Liora said, "I am drafting a fee schedule."

"This is about Noor."

Liora sat up.

"Alive?"

"Yes."

"Located?"

"Worse."

Ione placed the first wrist seal on the desk. Gold Sarqel edge, gray court
center, Aletheia refusal mark burned crooked across the glass.

"She tried to leave the south archive," Ione said. "The Sarqel Trust filed
emergency suitability review. Aletheia central filed protective silence. North
cloister filed total erasure. Three groups claimed to be saving her from the
other two."

"And you?"

"I ran out of theology."

Ione placed the second seal beside the first.

Blue. Child claimant.

Tamas.

Liora was awake now.

"Where is he?"

"With Mara."

That answer was so unexpected it took Liora a second to understand it.

"Mara has him?"

"Mara asked for him before the Ledger Houses could turn him into a symbol. His
mother agreed. The Court has not."

The wall docket woke.

```text
SEALED SET 19
STATUS: FLOOD ACTIVE
COLLISION DOMAINS: MARA VEYR / NOOR ARAM / TAMAS ELIAN DAUR
```

No counts. No parade of numbers. Just three names and the fact that the system
had found more than one usable version of each.

Liora opened the docket.

The first graph was not Mara.

It was a woman called Sela Daur, Tamas's mother, standing in a corridor
outside intake with rain in her hair and one hand pressed over her son's
bracelet. Her graph had not been included because she was recoverable. She was
included because every claimant needed a living handle.

The Null-Key Cells had not hidden that.

They had highlighted it.

"They are not only attacking the Court," Liora said.

"No," Ione said. "They are accusing it."

"By flooding children into evidence."

"By showing the children were already there."

That was the part Liora hated. Villains made work easier. Whistleblowers made
clean work impossible.

The central evidence hall had been built for spectacle and renamed for
procedure. By 04:40 it held three judges, five advocates, two restoration
mathematicians, a sealed public gallery, twenty-one frozen appeal channels,
Mara Veyr, Noor Aram, Tamas and his mother, Ione, and enough compatible selves
to make every person in the hall feel undercounted.

The floor showed no army of pale bodies this time.

Liora had ordered the projections collapsed into witness rings. One ring for
Mara. One for Noor. One for Tamas. One for Sela Daur. Within each ring, graphs
appeared as layered lines: memory, kinship, custody, residue, signature,
omission, coercion risk.

It was less dramatic.

It was more frightening.

The House Tabularii advocate objected immediately.

"The collapsed view suppresses evidentiary richness."

"Yes," Liora said.

"That disadvantages claimants."

"It also keeps children from being displayed as possible keys."

The judge on the left rail said, "Auditor Sen, classification."

"No."

The word crossed the hall too fast.

Liora corrected herself.

"Not yet."

Mara stood near the Tamas ring with one hand on the boy's shoulder. She did not
hold him possessively. She held him the way a person braces a door from a wind
that has learned law.

Noor stood inside her own ring because no one had told her not to and everyone
was too busy being horrified to correct her.

"This is what the Trust wants?" Noor asked.

Voss, the Sarqel advocate, looked offended by being made literal.

"The Trust wants lawful continuity."

Noor pointed to a line in her ring.

```text
NOOR ARAM: RESTORED CONSENT CONTEXT - SUITABILITY IMPROVEMENT PATH
```

"That says you can improve me until I agree."

"It says prior context can be reviewed for impairment."

"That is a long coat for a short knife."

Mara looked at Noor. "Do not let them make you answer every version."

Noor's jaw tightened.

"I was going to tell them to drown in every version."

"That also works."

Tamas laughed once, then looked guilty for making sound in a court that had
forgotten he was a child.

The registrar read the Null-Key receipt into record.

```text
YOU ASKED THE DEAD FOR CLEAN PROOF.
WE RETURNED THE MESS.
```

The Tabularii advocate rose.

"The packets are malicious."

Ione answered before Liora could stop her.

"They are curated."

The hall turned.

There it was: the split, no longer philosophical. Ione had said the useful
word in public.

The judge leaned forward. "Custodian?"

Ione looked tired now. Humanly tired. That was worse than mystery.

"The Null-Key Cells selected collision domains already being exploited. They
did not invent the appetite. They made it visible."

"You endorse sabotage?"

"I am saying the wound predates the knife."

Voss smiled. "Aletheia admits contact with the attackers."

"Aletheia admits disagreement with itself," Ione said.

Liora wanted to fine everyone in the room for honesty without timing.

The Mara ring flickered.

A red layer appeared.

```text
SEALED LETTER: HIGH COMPATIBILITY
STATUS: LOCATION CLAIMED / TEXT UNREAD
```

Elias Veyr, seated behind the advocate line, went pale.

Mara saw him before she read the line.

"You knew," she said.

Elias stood. "I knew there was something hidden. I did not know what."

"You restored a world to find me and did not lift a kitchen tile?"

He had no answer that did not sound like one.

The red layer pulsed again.

The court wanted the letter. Every advocate in the room wanted the letter.
Every engine wanted the letter because missing text is an insult to machines
trained on completion. Even Liora wanted it, and that was how she knew desire
could wear a badge.

Mara stepped into the ring.

"No display."

The mathematician said, "Dr. Veyr, the letter may materially affect continuity
review."

"Then the review can remain poorer."

"You do not know whether the letter helps you."

"That is not the same as consent."

The room did not like that. The room was built to make more information feel
like justice.

The Tamas ring brightened.

Sela Daur pulled her son closer.

"Why is he still in this?" she asked.

No one answered quickly enough.

The graph showed the answer: a low-resolution custody chain, a Ledger advocate
contact, two possible witness roles, one possible child keyholder path, and an
old recovery subsidy that had used his district as a test population.

Tamas did not understand all of it.

He understood enough to hide half his face behind Mara's sleeve.

The Last Archive remained silent.

That silence had weight.

Liora opened the black case.

`RECOVERABLE / NOT CLAIMABLE` was not enough. It named the returned person but
not the living people evidence tried to recruit.

`ACCESS != AUTHORITY` was not enough. The room had more than access now.

`NO CONSENT BY ANALOGY` was not enough. The analogy had become a flood.

The problem was not that proof had failed.

Proof was succeeding too well.

Every additional compatible graph gave someone another angle to claim, improve,
protect, complete, inherit, display, or litigate a person who had not asked to
be multiplied.

Liora took the uncarved block from the warm slot.

Laur instructors said never to invent law in public. Laur history was a list of
people inventing law in public and then being denounced by committees named
after them.

She pressed the block into ink and wrote on the docket by hand.

```text
PLURALITY IS NOT CONSENT.
```

The docket rejected the mark.

Liora wrote it again.

Mara read it first.

Noor second.

Sela third.

The judge last.

"Auditor Sen," the judge said, "what does that restrict?"

"Replacement. Completion. Correction. Multiplication. Display. Derivative
claim. Any use of compatible selves to overrule a claimant, a living dependent,
or an unregistered household."

"That is broader than the matter before us."

Liora looked at the four witness rings.

"No. The matter before us is broader than the Court admitted."

The Last Archive answered at last.

```text
FIELD MARK RECEIVED.
STATUS: NECESSARY / INSUFFICIENT.
```

Insufficient.

The honest word.

The projections did not vanish. They paused. The rings held their layered
lines like wounds cauterized too early.

Voss began writing appeal language before the pause completed.

The Tabularii advocate began writing exceptions.

Ione began crying without changing her expression.

Noor saw it and looked away, offering the only mercy available.

Mara took Tamas's hand from her sleeve and placed it in his mother's hand.

"He is not mine," Mara said.

Sela nodded once.

"Thank you."

"Do not thank me for not stealing your child."

"In this room," Sela said, "I will thank anyone for that."

The line did more damage than any graph.

Liora sealed the mark.

The wall added a next question.

```text
WHO MAY BENEFIT FROM A PERSON'S COMPATIBLE SELVES?
```

Every advocate looked up.

That was how Liora knew the morning had not been won.

It had merely been named.

Outside the evidence hall, the court corridors were filling with people whose
cases had just become less simple. Families, brokers, cloister clerks, trust
agents, children with colored bracelets, restored adults with no language for
why more proof made them feel less real.

The Null-Key Cells sent one final line to the sealed docket.

```text
YOU CALL THIS FLOOD.
WE CALL IT THE SHAPE OF YOUR HUNGER.
```

Liora did not enter it as doctrine.

She entered it as evidence of motive.

Then she locked the black case and held it with both hands until the stamps
stopped trembling.
