## Episode Function

Episode 1 made restoration ethically unusable.

Episode 2 makes the Genesis Chain legally dangerous. It shows that old
cryptographic authority did not disappear when price died. It became a relic
economy, then a succession technology, then a method for turning living people
into the heirs of dead commands.

## Revised English Draft

### The Dead Exchange

The Dead Exchange opened three hours after the court closed.

It had no bell, no market board, no shouting clerks, and no prices.

That was what the tour guides said, because tour guides were paid to preserve
the dignity of lies. Liora Sen knew better. The Dead Exchange had prices. It
had merely outlived the vulgar kind.

No one bought coins there. No one sold coins. No screen showed a rising number
or a falling one. The last public price of the Genesis Chain had been embalmed
in schoolbooks, argued over by small sects, and then made useless by the only
market event that mattered.

The keys had returned.

After that, price became an embarrassment. The old believers had asked whether
the number would ever reach zero. The century answered by making the question
provincial. A crown did not need to buy bread. A crown needed only to be
recognized by people willing to kill, marry, sue, inherit, defect, or pray
under it.

The Dead Exchange traded recognition.

It lived beneath the eastern vaults of the Court of Continuance, below the
public galleries, below the intake halls, below the waiting rooms where families
learned to speak of the dead in admissible grammar. The public entrance was a
museum arch. The real entrance was a freight lift behind Chamber Two, opened
only by court salt, Laur sleeve-thread, and the kind of exhausted authority that
looked like permission to machines.

Liora stood before the lift with the new docket still tacky in her case.

```text
MARAE VEYR
RECOVERABLE / NOT CLAIMABLE
TEMPORARY AUDITOR MARK
```

Temporary, the clerk had insisted.

Auditor mark, the judge had warned.

Unauthorized, House Tabularii had said six times in four different registers.

Mara had said nothing.

That had been the hardest part. Liora knew how to read objection, panic, fraud,
relief, contempt, and institutional appetite. She did not know how to read the
face of a restored woman who had been spared one claim and delivered to a
larger machine.

The freight lift opened.

An Aletheia custodian waited inside.

She had not been in Chamber Two, but her veil carried the same undecorated
plainness as the observer who had interrupted the hearing. The Aletheia Veil
had many uniforms and only one insult: it made every other uniform look as
though it were begging to be noticed.

"Auditor Sen," the custodian said.

"I do not remember requesting a witness."

"You requested a conscience. The court forwarded the need."

Liora stepped into the lift. "The court has never forwarded a conscience."

"Then consider me misdelivered."

The lift descended.

For a while there was only the soft complaint of old machinery and the custodian
breathing through linen.

"Name?" Liora asked.

"Ione."

"Common or given?"

"Permitted."

That was either a joke or an answer. Aletheia people were fond of statements
that could survive as both.

The lift passed the archive humidity line. Liora felt it in her throat before
the indicator changed. Paper, salt, cooled stone, old insulation, and something
faintly metallic: the smell of institutions that had learned to call rot by
more expensive names.

"You objected to the Genesis Chain precedent," Ione said.

"I objected to using a wallet as a widow."

"That was not the dangerous part."

"I know."

The lift stopped.

The Dead Exchange spread below the court like a drowned city.

It had once been a coin vault, then a museum, then a sealed evidence annex, then
a custody tribunal, then a place with no honest name left. Its ceiling was too
low for grandeur and too high for comfort. Long aisles cut through rows of black
tables. Each table held a chain fragment under glass: a dead address, a
reconstructed signature, a custody tree, a claim history, an objection history,
and the genealogy of everyone who had ever tried to make the fragment mean
something again.

There were no traders.

There were advocates.

That was worse.

At the nearest table, a white-haired woman in mourning blue argued with two
Ledger clerks over a signature made four hundred years before her birth. At the
next, three brothers sat in silence while a court reader explained that their
father's restored key had transferred a salt estate to a monastery he had
despised. Farther down, a family line had split into six legal subfamilies
around an address that had never held enough ancient value to buy a room in the
city it now governed.

Dead money still produced living posture.

People leaned toward it. People lowered their voices around it. People said
words like proof, chain, priority, and continuity as though the old machines had
been priests.

"This is not an exchange," Ione said.

"It is exactly an exchange."

"Nothing is traded."

"That is the clerks' theory."

Liora passed the first row. Every table had a silence around it. Not peace.
Silence. The shape left when people agreed that the important thing could not
be named too directly.

"They trade blame," Liora said. "They trade permission. They trade who must
become the kind of person a dead record requires."

Ione did not answer.

The escort clerk found them halfway down the central aisle. He was young enough
to be proud of working underground and old enough to hide it badly.

"Auditor Sen. The House requested immediate review."

"Which house?"

"Domus Tabularii, northern register."

"They requested many things today."

"This one concerns the Veyr precedent."

Of course it did.

The clerk guided them past the custody tables to a sealed alcove marked only
with a number.

```text
S-0 / DERIVATIVE CLAIM
```

Derivative. Liora hated the word. It made violence sound like a footnote.

Inside the alcove, five people waited around a black table.

The House Tabularii advocate from the hearing stood nearest the exhibit,
already arranged into grievance. Beside him was a restoration mathematician in
grey gloves, a civil registrar, an elderly man wearing the bronze collar of an
old custody line, and a living heir.

The living heir was the problem. Liora knew before anyone spoke.

She was perhaps nineteen, though legal age looked unreliable in the restoration
century. Thin, severe, and underdressed for an inheritance fight, she stood with
her hands flat against the table as if she were preventing herself from being
moved by the exhibit's gravity.

Her bracelet was white.

Not claimant.

Witness.

Good, Liora thought.

Then she corrected herself.

No. Not good.

Witnesses could be converted.

The House advocate bowed with the minimum compatible with insult.

"Auditor Sen. Your new mark has created confusion."

"Then it is functioning."

The registrar cleared his throat. "We are here for the Sarqel Fragment."

The black table woke.

Under the glass lay nothing dramatic: a storage wafer, a custody seal, a strip
of brittle paper printed with obsolete characters, and a line of reconstructed
signature text. The chain address itself appeared on the wall above it in
ritual formatting.

No one needed the formatting.

Everyone wanted it.

```text
GENESIS CHAIN: SARQEL FRAGMENT
STATUS: KEY RECONSTRUCTED
ACCESS: VERIFIED
SUCCESSION FIELD: DISPUTED
```

"State the issue," Liora said.

The advocate touched the table. The wall changed.

```text
RECOVERED KEY AUTHORIZES TRANSFER OF CUSTODY CLAIM
DESIGNATED HEIR: NOOR ARAM
HEIR STATUS: LIVING
HEIR RESPONSE: DENIAL
```

The young woman did not blink.

"Noor Aram?" Liora asked.

"Yes."

"You deny the designation?"

"I deny everything that sentence wants from me."

That was a better answer than yes.

Liora opened her case.

The House advocate stiffened.

"The key is verified," he said quickly. "Access reconstruction passed chain
integrity and witness-context thresholds. The signature is compatible with the
Sarqel line's lost custody branch. The transfer names Ms. Aram by civic name
and private salt index."

Noor's hand tightened against the table.

Private salt index.

The words changed the room. Even the registrar looked down.

"Who supplied the salt index?" Liora asked.

"Recovered from the chain context," said the mathematician.

"That was not my question."

The mathematician folded his grey-gloved hands. "No living party supplied it."

"Did Ms. Aram?"

"No."

"Did her family?"

"No."

"Did the Sarqel line?"

"No surviving file establishes that."

Liora selected a stamp without looking.

ASSUMED.

The advocate put one hand over the docket before she could press it.

That was bold. Also stupid.

Ione moved first.

She did not touch him. She only looked at his hand until he remembered the
number of cameras in the alcove and removed it.

Liora stamped the docket.

ASSUMED.

"The access is verified," the advocate repeated.

"Access to what?"

"The key."

"And the key proves what?"

"Continuity of custody."

"Custody of what?"

The advocate paused.

The old man in the bronze collar answered instead.

"A seat."

Noor laughed.

It was nothing like Mara's laugh. Mara had laughed because the room had finally
said something obscene plainly enough to hear. Noor laughed because the room had
not.

"A seat," she said.

The old man looked at her with something like pity. "A voting seat in the
Sarqel Trust. It is not ornamental."

"My grandmother burned the Trust letters."

"Your grandmother was not the designated heir."

"My grandmother was the one who kept us out of this sewer."

The registrar flinched. The Dead Exchange disliked being named accurately.

Liora looked at the custody tree. Sarqel line, broken branch, recovery branch,
Noor Aram. The tree was beautiful. That was usually a sign that someone had
removed the ugly parts.

"Why is House Tabularii involved?" she asked.

The advocate recovered his expression. "The Trust holds dormant rights in three
continuity archives, two court-grade restoration engines, and a minority vote
in the Veyr derivative review."

There it was.

Mara again.

Not directly. Never directly. Power rarely walked through the front door after
being told to wait outside. It bought a neighboring room and removed the wall.

"So a dead key names a living heir," Liora said, "and the living heir receives
authority over the archive engines that may help decide whether Mara Veyr can
remain unclaimed."

"That is an inflammatory summary."

"It is a shorter one."

Noor looked at Liora for the first time with something sharper than fear.

"I do not want the seat."

"Refusal recorded," said the registrar too quickly.

"No," Ione said.

Everyone turned.

The Aletheia custodian had not moved from the door. In the exchange light her
veil was almost the same color as the glass over the dead key.

"Refusal is not recorded by hearing the sentence," Ione said. "Refusal is a
right with a custody chain of its own."

The registrar said, "The witness is not a party."

"That is the point."

The House advocate gave Liora a look that meant: control your conscience.

Liora did not.

"Ms. Aram," she said, "do you know how your private salt index entered the
reconstruction?"

"No."

"Did you ever submit it to a civic archive?"

"No."

"Religious archive? Medical archive? School archive? Debt archive?"

"No."

"Family witness chain?"

Noor's mouth changed.

Not much. Enough.

"My mother did."

The old man in the bronze collar closed his eyes.

Liora waited.

Good law, when it existed, was mostly waiting with consequences.

Noor said, "She thought the records were for burial insurance. Low-resolution
only. She was afraid I would disappear if she died first."

The wall did not change. Machines rarely knew when a sentence mattered.

"And the Sarqel Fragment used that salt index?"

The mathematician said, "The reconstruction incorporated compatible civic
residue from multiple sources."

Liora stamped the second line.

CIRCULAR IF FITTED.

The advocate made a small sound of offense.

"Auditor Sen, this is not a theory hearing."

"Everything in this building is a theory hearing. The dead simply make better
exhibits."

She turned to the mathematician.

"Did the reconstruction find Noor Aram as heir, or did the model search for a
living heir compatible with a desired custody recovery?"

"That distinction is not meaningful under the current access standard."

"Then the current access standard is not meaningful."

Noor stared at the wall where her name remained clean, formal, already partly
taken.

```text
DESIGNATED HEIR: NOOR ARAM
```

"Remove my name," she said.

The registrar folded his hands. "The record cannot remove a recovered
designation."

"Then mark that I refuse it."

"The Trust charter treats refusal as temporary incapacity pending counsel."

"I am not incapacitated."

"You are nineteen."

"I am old enough to be conscripted by a dead key."

No one corrected her.

Liora felt the episode forming around her, though she would not have used that
word. Cases had weather. Some arrived as rain. Some as pressure. This one had
the smell of a storm still deciding where to break.

She looked again at the table.

The Sarqel Fragment was smaller than the fear it produced. That was true of
most gods.

"Show me the signature context," she said.

The mathematician hesitated.

"Now."

The wall changed.

Lines of recovered transaction grammar unfolded, too old to be beautiful and
too consequential to be ugly. Most of it meant nothing to anyone without
training. Liora had enough training to dislike what she saw.

The key had not simply transferred custody.

It had anticipated dispute.

```text
IF DESIGNATED HEIR REFUSES:
  HOLD IN TRUST UNTIL HEIR'S CONTINUITY CAN BE CONFIRMED
IF HEIR'S CONTINUITY IS DISPUTED:
  SEEK ARCHIVAL RESTORATION OF PRIOR CONSENT CONTEXT
```

Noor read the wall.

"What does that mean?"

The old man in the bronze collar did not answer.

The advocate did.

"It means the Trust is obligated to protect the integrity of your claim."

"No," Liora said.

The word came out before she chose it.

The room registered the breach. Auditors were supposed to ask, mark, classify,
delay. They were not supposed to translate danger before counsel had time to
rename it.

Noor turned to her.

Liora made herself continue.

"It means if you refuse the seat, the Trust can argue your refusal is evidence
that your continuity has been damaged. Then they can petition to restore the
prior consent context that would make you willing."

For a moment the Dead Exchange was honest.

The advocates looked like advocates.

The registrar looked like a clerk of capture.

The old man looked old.

Noor looked nineteen.

Ione lowered her head.

"Consent cannot be inherited like a key," she said.

The House advocate spoke softly now. Softly was worse.

"No one is proposing coercion."

"You are proposing grammar," Liora said. "Coercion's older sibling."

The Last Archive did not answer.

That silence mattered. It meant the machine was listening, or absent, or
waiting for humans to become guilty enough to be legible.

Liora closed her case.

"The Sarqel Fragment is suspended."

"On what authority?" the advocate asked.

"Mine, for the next twelve hours."

"And after twelve hours?"

"You may find a judge willing to explain why a living heir must be repaired
until she agrees with a dead key."

The old man in the bronze collar touched the edge of the glass.

"You do not understand what you are stopping."

Liora believed him. That was the problem. Villains were easy. Believers with
good records were the ones who built infrastructure.

"Then write it down," she said. "Preferably before you call it love."

Noor stepped back from the table.

No machine stopped her.

That was how Liora knew the system had not expected anyone to walk away.

At the alcove door, Noor paused.

"Auditor."

Liora looked at her.

"If they can restore the consent context that would make me accept, is that
still me?"

It was the same question Mara had refused, wearing a younger face.

Liora could have answered as a Laur officer. She could have said unknown,
pending, inadmissible, disputed, outside scope. She could have offered a
sentence that survived appeal by giving no one anything to hold.

Instead she looked at the wall.

```text
DESIGNATED HEIR: NOOR ARAM
```

Then at Noor.

"That is what they want the question to be."

Ione's veil shifted.

Noor understood enough to be more afraid.

"What is the question?"

Liora did not have the Last Archive's voice. She had ink, delay, and twelve
hours.

Sometimes law was only a place to stand while the real danger learned your
name.

"Who benefits," Liora said, "from needing you repaired?"

The Dead Exchange did not close after they left.

It did not sleep. It did not mourn the markets that had once made it loud. It
kept its black tables lit and its custody trees watered and its relics under
glass, because a dead chain could not desire power.

Only its heirs could do that for it.

In the freight lift, Ione finally removed one glove.

Her palm bore a witness mark Liora recognized from the oldest Aletheia files:
not a family sign, not a religious vow, not a court credential.

A refusal chain.

"Noor Aram will not be the last," Ione said.

"No."

"Mara Veyr was not the first."

Liora looked at the mark.

"No."

The lift rose.

Above them, the Court of Continuance prepared tomorrow's docket. Families were
already sleeping in rows beside vending machines, holding names in their mouths
like coins they were afraid to spend. Somewhere in the city, Elias Veyr was
learning that losing a claim was not the same as losing the machinery that had
made the claim possible.

Somewhere else, Noor Aram was alive and newly useful.

The lift doors opened.

The clerk from Chamber Two waited with another docket.

Liora did not take it.

"Say it."

The clerk swallowed.

"The Last Archive has classified the Sarqel Fragment as related to the Veyr
case."

"It is related."

"Not procedurally."

"Then how?"

The clerk looked at Ione, then at Liora, then at the floor.

"Civilization-scale consent artifact."

The phrase passed through the hallway like a cold draft.

Liora almost laughed.

Not because it was funny. Because the century had finally found a way to make
an old coin more frightening after it stopped being money.

She took the docket.

On the last page, beneath the custody tree and the recovered key and Noor
Aram's unwanted name, the Last Archive had added one line.

```text
THE CHAIN WAS DEAD.
ITS HEIRS WERE NOT.
```

Liora read it twice.

Then she opened the black case and made a new mark beside the line.

```text
ACCESS != AUTHORITY
```

The ink dried quickly this time.

That worried her more.
