## Exhibit S-0

By noon, the Waiting City had run out of quiet.

Rain came down through the open ribs of the transit hall and broke against the
heat shields in silver dust. The dead stood under the awnings with numbered
bracelets. The living stood beside them, behind them, in front of them, touching
their sleeves too often and pretending not to count the seconds between each
breath.

Liora Sen had spent nine years in intake and still disliked the word dead.

The Court of Continuance used cleaner terms. Recovered persons. Displaced
continuities. Petition-bearing identities. The language had been designed by
people who never had to tell a child that the grandmother in front of him had
been assembled from planetary weather, hospital dust, ancestral backups,
black-market prayer engines, and a legal warrant older than his house.

Liora preferred names.

At Station S-0, the name for the next hearing blinked in red across the glass.

```text
MARA VEYR
RECOVERY STATUS: STABLE
CLAIMANTS: ONE
EXHIBIT CLASS: GENESIS-CHAIN DERIVATIVE
```

The clerk beside Liora looked at the last line and made a small sound in his
throat.

"Do not make that face," Liora said.

"I didn't make a face."

"You made the Genesis face."

"Everyone makes the Genesis face."

He was young enough to think the first chain had been born as a myth. Most
clerks were. They knew the story in fragments: twenty-first-century miners,
private keys, old fortunes, dead exchanges, treaties written by machines that
had outlived their signers, and the strange religious embarrassment of a
civilization that could recover almost anything except why it had worshiped
scarcity.

Liora tapped the glass until the file opened.

"Genesis derivatives are property language," she said. "This is a continuity
hearing."

"The claimant says the property language proves continuity."

"Claimants say many things."

Through the glass, the Waiting City turned another bracelet green. A woman in a
borrowed coat fell to her knees before a restored man and laughed as if someone
had cut a rope inside her. The restored man looked past her at the wet ceiling.
He had not yet learned which grief was supposed to be his.

Liora closed Mara Veyr's file and took the long corridor to Chamber Two.

The corridor had been built to calm petitioners. It failed. Its walls displayed
old decisions in soft light: first child restored from atmospheric residue,
first tyrant denied sovereign reentry, first monastery granted collective
forgetting, first marriage voided after duplicate recovery. The cases drifted
past in polished fragments. No one read them. People only searched for a line
that sounded like permission.

At the chamber door, a broker waited with two assistants and a case of dead
hardware.

He was dressed in mourning gray, though no one in the file had died recently
enough to make that honest. His name was Pell Anson, licensed succession broker,
second-tier Ledger House advocate, and the sort of man who smiled at procedure
because he believed procedure could be bought wholesale if one arrived early
enough.

"Auditor Sen," he said. "You have our gratitude for taking this directly."

"You do not have my gratitude for blocking the corridor."

His smile adjusted by one degree.

"The exhibit is sensitive."

"Then stop waving it at foot traffic."

One assistant stiffened. The other did not look up. The case between them was
transparent and vacuum sealed. Inside lay a black device no larger than Liora's
palm, cracked along one edge, its casing scarred by heat and salt. A strip of
court paper had been looped around it.

```text
EXHIBIT S-0: BROKEN CROWN
ACCESS RECOVERED: PARTIAL
AUTHORITY CLAIMED: PENDING
```

Pell touched the case with two fingers.

"The object was carried by Elias Veyr at the time of the Salt Fires. It
contains a derived access path tied to pre-collapse Bitcoin custody. The
reconstruction engines recovered a valid signature chain. The same path
identified Mara Veyr as lawful counterparty, spouse, and intended beneficiary."

Liora looked at him.

"That was the short version?"

"That was the version that respects your time."

"Try the version that respects the person in the next room."

For the first time, Pell's smile forgot its own shape.

"The person in the next room exists because the claimant paid for a restoration
petition."

"That explains why she is here. It does not explain what she owes."

The chamber door opened before Pell could answer.

Mara Veyr stood barefoot on the threshold.

She had refused the white court robe. Someone had given her intake trousers and
a sweater with the sleeves rolled three times. Her hair was still damp from the
stabilization bath, and the fine tremor in her hands had not been tuned out.
Restoration made bodies clean and inaccurate in the first hours, as if the
universe could remember a person but not their preferred discomfort.

Behind her stood Elias Veyr.

He was older than the archival photographs, thinner, and composed with visible
effort. Grief had not ruined his face. It had trained it. He looked at Mara with
the fixed attention of a man trying to keep a door open by staring at it.

Mara looked at the case.

"No," she said.

The word fell flat into the corridor.

Pell lifted both hands. "Madam Veyr, this is not the decision stage."

"Good," Mara said. "Then no one should need a decision from me."

Elias took one step forward.

"Mara."

She did not turn.

Liora watched the broker, the assistants, the restored woman, the husband, the
dead device. The whole corridor tightened around a grammar everyone knew by
habit: access, recovery, claim.

"Chamber," Liora said.

Inside, Chamber Two looked less like justice than maintenance. The benches were
scratched. The translation lamps buzzed. Someone had left a paper cup beneath
the respondent table. The Court of Continuance had ceremonial halls for public
rulings; intake chambers were where the machinery admitted it still needed
people to push it.

Liora liked them for that reason.

She took the auditor's desk. Pell and his assistants arranged the Broken Crown
as if placing a relic. Elias remained standing until Mara sat. Mara chose the
chair farthest from him.

The court opened with no anthem. Chamber Two did not waste music on people
whose lives had already been interrupted.

"Mara Veyr," Liora said, "do you understand your present status before the
Court?"

"No."

Pell shifted.

Liora marked the answer.

"Good," she said. "Then we will not pretend you do."

Elias closed his eyes. It might have been relief.

"You have been recovered under a continuity petition submitted by Elias Veyr,"
Liora continued. "The petition is attached to Exhibit S-0, a broken custody
device now called the Broken Crown. The claimant asserts that recovered access
to the device establishes lawful authority to complete your reentry."

Mara touched the cuffs of her sweater.

"Complete me into what?"

"That is one of the questions."

"No," Mara said again.

This time Liora looked up.

"Record the respondent's refusal."

Pell rose halfway. "Auditor, refusal cannot be entered before capacity review."

"It can be entered as sound."

"Sound is not a legal category."

"It is when everyone in the room heard it."

The chamber system hesitated, then printed:

```text
RESPONDENT UTTERANCE: REFUSAL
LEGAL WEIGHT: UNDETERMINED
```

Mara looked at the line as if it were the first honest object she had seen.

Pell sat down.

Liora turned to Elias. "Claimant statement."

Elias placed both hands on the table. He did not look at Pell.

"She is my wife," he said.

The simple sentences were always the dangerous ones.

"Was," Mara said.

The room went still.

Elias flinched, but not away from her. Toward her.

"I waited," he said. "I kept the estate sealed. I refused remarriage. I refused
proxy companionship. I refused every settlement that required declaring you
complete."

"You declared me recoverable."

"Because you were."

"That is not the same thing."

Pell's expression sharpened. Liora saw him hear value enter the room. Brokers
loved distinctions. Distinctions could be priced, argued, inherited, converted
into chairs on boards that no living founder had ever imagined.

"The claimant does not argue possession," Pell said. "He argues restoration of
interrupted rights."

Mara laughed once. It hurt to hear.

"He restored a key and found a wife attached to it."

"Madam Veyr," Pell said, "the key did not restore you. The key proved priority."

"Priority to what?"

Pell glanced at Liora, annoyed to be made plain.

"To initiate reentry procedure."

"Into his house?"

"Into continuity."

"That is a nicer room?"

Liora let the silence lengthen. Procedure was most revealing when denied rescue.

The Broken Crown projected its record above the table. Not the old chain itself;
no one trusted raw antiquity anymore. The court displayed a layered
reconstruction: signatures, derived custody paths, salt-damaged backups,
marriage contracts, emergency wills, neural residue, household sensor dust,
letters Mara had written and Elias had never answered because she had vanished
before they arrived.

Most of it was admissible.

That was the horror.

The engine did not lie. Not in the childish sense. It found correlations, tested
noise, cross-checked witnesses, repaired missing context, and returned a woman
who matched Mara Veyr better than any fraud could. Liora had spent years trying
to explain that accuracy was not mercy. The court preferred shorter phrases.

"Capacity review," Liora said.

The chamber dimmed. Three questions appeared in the air.

```text
NAME?
LAST FREE INTENTION?
CLAIMED RELATION?
```

Mara read them.

"Mara Veyr. I was leaving. He was my husband."

Elias bowed his head.

The system printed green across all three.

Pell exhaled.

Mara watched the color and did not move.

"That is enough?" she asked.

"For baseline continuity," Liora said. "Not for disposition."

"Disposition," Mara said. "Like a room. Like an asset."

"Like a case," Liora said.

Mara's mouth tightened. "Better."

Pell came forward again. He had regained his rhythm.

"The respondent recognizes the claimant. The continuity engine confirms stable
identity. The device ties recovery to a lawful petition. We ask the Court to
advance to provisional spousal reentry."

"Denied," Liora said.

Pell blinked. "On what ground?"

Liora did not yet have one.

That was the problem.

The court had categories for failed recovery, fraud, duplicate conflict,
capacity delay, criminal contamination, sovereign exclusion, monastic erasure,
and market misconduct. It did not have a clean category for a person who was
probably who she was, whose claimant may have loved her, whose recovery may have
been technically valid, and who still stood in the room like a locked door no
key had the right to admire.

Liora opened the manual anyway.

The manual offered nothing useful.

Across the chamber, the Last Archive noticed.

It never announced itself dramatically. The Archive corrected questions the way
old buildings corrected footsteps: with pressure, not speech. A second line
appeared beneath the court's status field.

```text
QUESTION DEFECT DETECTED
```

Pell went pale with irritation.

Elias looked frightened.

Mara looked almost amused.

Liora kept her hands still.

"Archive," she said, "state the defect."

The lamps dimmed once.

```text
COURT ASKS: IS SHE RECOVERED?
UNASKED: WHO MAY CLAIM WHAT RECOVERY RETURNS?
```

Liora stared at the line.

There it was. Too clean to be doctrine. Too useful to ignore.

Pell recovered first.

"The Archive is advisory."

"So am I," Liora said.

"Auditor Sen."

"Sit down."

The words came out before she had decided to spend them. The chamber recorded
them, because chambers were malicious about courage.

Liora took a field slate from the drawer. It was meant for intake anomalies:
wrong dates, incomplete kinship, uncertain language, minor fraud. Not for
Genesis derivatives. Not for a hearing that half the Ledger Houses were already
watching through sealed mirrors.

She wrote:

```text
RECOVERABLE / NOT CLAIMABLE
```

The slate refused the category.

She wrote it again.

The slate accepted it as provisional because old systems feared repeated human
input more than error.

Pell stood. "That mark has no standing."

"Correct."

"Then it cannot bind the parties."

"Also correct."

"Then what is it?"

Liora placed the slate beside the Broken Crown.

"A delay."

Elias looked at her as if she had taken something from him.

Mara looked at her as if she had given her nothing and therefore perhaps
something.

Liora addressed the chamber.

"Mara Veyr is provisionally classified as recoverable and not claimable.
Baseline continuity may proceed. Spousal reentry, estate activation, custody
assignment, beneficiary transfer, and any derivative authority from Exhibit S-0
are stayed pending doctrine review."

Pell's assistants began sending messages.

"On whose authority?" Pell asked.

Liora looked at the Broken Crown. Then at Mara.

"Mine, until someone with more authority arrives to make a worse mistake."

The chamber printed the stay.

Mara closed her eyes.

Elias whispered, "I brought you back."

Mara opened them again.

"You brought back enough of me to hear no."

He had no answer.

The Last Archive added one more line, uninvited.

```text
LOVE IS NOT A KEY.
```

For once, no one objected to the grammar.

Outside, the Waiting City called another number. Somewhere in the rain, another
family learned that recovery was not return, not yet, not always, and not for
free.

Liora signed the stay before she could become wiser.

When the chamber doors opened, the broker did not look angry anymore.

He looked interested.

That worried her more.
