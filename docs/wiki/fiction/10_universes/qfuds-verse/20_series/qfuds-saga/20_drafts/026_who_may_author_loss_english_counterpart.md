## Who May Author Loss

The day after the ruling, the first person outside the Court to make money was
not an undertaker.

It was a seal-maker.

The square had stopped raining before dawn, and now the wet tents stood in
rows. Each one carried black ribbons, wrist seals, small metal plates, cheap
charms made to hang from a child's schoolbag. Words that had lived only on
exhibit tags the day before had, overnight, become things.

```text
RECOVERABLE / NOT CLAIMABLE
ACCESS != AUTHORITY
NO CONSENT BY ANALOGY
PLURALITY IS NOT CONSENT
PHYSICS IS NOT JURISDICTION
```

One seller offered bracelets with all five lines cut into a single band.
Another shrank `protected pending` down to the size of a wedding ring and slid
it onto people's fingers. A boy who could not even read the ruling ran around
with `NOT CLAIMABLE` stuck to his chest. His mother wore the face of someone who
believed the sentence would keep him safe.

It was that face that kept Liora Sen from holding on to her anger.

That was the problem.

Power did not always arrive wearing a bad face. Sometimes it came in wet
sneakers, with hot hands and sleepless eyes, with a mouth that said, "Give us
one too." The sentences the Court had barely managed to pin down yesterday had,
by this morning, become as necessary to people as umbrellas.

It was the market that had sold the umbrellas.

Liora crossed the square. The black coat of Bureau Laurien did almost nothing
against the rain. What it did instead was part the crowd. The sellers recognized
her and lowered their voices. A few raised them.

"Same typeface as the official text!"

"Ruling-compatible seals!"

"For protecting family, for blocking creditors, for stopping a spouse's
claim!"

Liora stopped.

The seller was young. The metal plate in his hand had `RECOVERABLE / NOT
CLAIMABLE` stamped on it upside down. He knew only half of what he was selling.
The people who know half sell the fastest.

"Explain what 'for blocking creditors' means."

The young seller saw the collar of her coat and swallowed his grin.

"Just what it says. So that when a dead person comes back, they aren't dragged
off over a debt—"

"Who guarantees that?"

"The Court—"

"The Court has never issued such a product."

"But yesterday—"

He swallowed the rest. He had seen the black case in Liora's hand. Inside it
were the real seals. Heavy ones. The kind that were only ever any use set down
beside a failed question.

The young seller slowly lowered the plate.

"People want them."

He was right.

That made it worse.

In front of the Court's main building hung a new sign.

```text
TEMPORARY LOSS AUTHORSHIP INTAKE
보호 미결 교리 관련 상실 작성 임시 접수
```

Liora looked at the third line for a long time.

Loss authoring.

Someone had translated `author` that way overnight. To write. To compose. To
become the author of. Neither was enough; neither was wrong. Words always took
on several meanings at the most dangerous moment.

At the entrance the line already stood three deep.

The first line was families. Photographs, urns, wrist seals, worn clothing,
pillowcases that still held a smell, petitions asking to cancel a restoration
request.

The second line was delegates. The black ledger-house crests of the Ledger
House, the thin document cases of the Trust, the white gloves of inheritance
lawyers, people who pronounced the names of the dead more precisely than they
pronounced the names of the living.

The third line was the people holding nothing at all.

It was hard to call them a line. They gathered along the base of the wall, never
given a ticket number, shoved back again and again by the guards. People with
nothing to hold were always the first to be doubted.

Liora walked toward the third line.

The clerk at the door stopped her.

"Auditor Sen. In the morning the loss-authoring intake handles only the
emergency family group."

"Who ordered that?"

"A temporary administrative order."

"Who wrote it?"

The clerk did not answer.

Liora hated that silence. It was hard to tell whether the silence was one
person's fear, an institution's training, or a blank already built into the
form.

The clerk spoke in a low voice.

"The square is dangerous. Since the ruling yesterday, requests have come in all
at once — restoration cancellations, restoration bans, inheritance blocks,
spousal-claim prevention, refusals of posthumous testimony. People want a mark
that says they've filed something."

"Even when they don't know what the mark is?"

"They want it because they don't know."

That, too, was true.

Liora gripped the case harder.

At the intake counter inside, a new form was glowing. Its title was three lines.

```text
LOSS AUTHORSHIP PETITION
AUTHORIZED AUTHOR CLASS:
SUBJECT RECOVERY INDEX:
```

Liora reached out and froze the screen.

"Good that it says subject and not respondent."

The clerk felt a very small relief.

"Thank you."

"But who approved the authorized author class?"

The relief vanished at once.

Below the form were the options.

```text
SPOUSE
DESCENDANT
REGISTERED CUSTODIAN
LEDGER HOUSE
STATE NECESSITY
ARCHIVE EXCEPTION
SELF, IF RECOVERED
```

Liora's finger stopped on the last line.

Self, if recovered.

Only once recovered could you become the author of your own loss.

For the unrecovered there was not even a blank.

"Close this form."

"It's already taking submissions."

"Close it."

"Auditor authority doesn't cover administrative forms—"

Liora looked at him.

He revised himself.

"A temporary suspension request is possible."

"Good. File a temporary suspension request."

"Under which item?"

The question was real. He was not resisting. He simply did not know which field
of the form to press in order to do a thing the form did not contain.

Then, from the third line, a woman made a sound.

It was not a scream. A scream does not wait its turn. This was the sound of
someone who had waited too long and was sparing her own throat.

"They get filed and we don't — why?"

A guard moved in front of her. The woman was short, in a wet gray coat. She held
a dented lunchbox in both arms. An old mine number was scratched into it. One
side of the lid had melted and would not close.

Liora came closer.

"Name."

"Sera Venn."

"Whose case?"

"My brother's. Oren Venn. He died in the Salt Fire."

At the words Salt Fire two clerks looked at each other. The records of that
disaster were filthy. The worker roster had been sold three times, the
evacuation ledger had been written after the fire, and the family notices had
gone out only after the company shut its doors. Restoration civilization was
always less proud in the face of an old death. A death with a lot of recoverable
data and a death with a lot of liability to compensate were not the same death.

Liora looked at the lunchbox.

"What's inside?"

Sera opened the lid.

Inside was a small spoon, black dust, and a piece of metal smaller than a palm.
Only part of a name was left on the metal.

```text
...REN V...
```

"Did he write that?"

"No. The company gave it. After he died."

"And the restoration request?"

"Filed three times."

"The result?"

Sera rubbed the metal piece with her thumb.

"Unsuitable. Low resolution. Insufficient subject identification. Possible
contamination by family memory. Equipment records lost at the time of the
accident. Damage during transfer of the company archive. The words were
different every time. The meaning was the same."

"What was the meaning?"

"That my brother died too poor to come back."

The sound of the square fell away.

Liora hated moments like that. The moment the world goes quiet. In stories that
moment looks important, but in reality it was dangerous. A world gone quiet
turns one person's words into a symbol far too easily.

Sera Venn was not a symbol.

She was someone inside a wet coat about to catch a cold, someone whose knuckles
had gone white keeping an old lunchbox from slipping out of her hands.

"What did you come to file?"

"Not a restoration ban."

"Then?"

"I came to have the loss acknowledged."

The clerk cut in.

"In that case the death-record correction counter—"

Without looking at him, Sera said:

"There are death records. Three of them. The company's, the district's, the
Court's. Every one of them spells my brother's name differently."

Liora stopped the clerk.

Sera went on.

"A Ledger House man came. He said he'd bought the compensation bond the company
left behind. He said if Oren were restored he'd gain the right to testify about
the cause of the accident, and that testimony right would help recover the bond.
If he isn't restored, the man asked me to consent to providing family memory. He
said selling the memory would raise the chance of restoration."

"Did you refuse?"

"Yes."

"What did they say?"

"That if the only way to prove my brother had truly existed was my memory, then
my memory might be a public interest."

Noor Aram's voice came from behind.

"Now I know where they heard that sentence."

Liora turned.

Noor stood with Ione. Noor had her back to the wall, the same stance she'd taken
when she refused the Sarqel seat. A body that trusted no chair. Ione had untied
her blue knot and wound it around her wrist. A sign that she would not fully
represent either side of the Aletheia.

"Why are you here?" Liora asked.

"To watch the words you wrote turn into price tags," Noor said.

"The words I wrote are not price tags."

"That's why I came to watch."

Ione looked at Sera's lunchbox.

"The same fight broke out inside the Aletheia. One side wants a refusal
registry. Record your refusal of restoration in advance, while you're still
alive. The other side holds that the moment a registry exists, the people who
failed to register become owned by default."

Sera asked:

"And the ones who couldn't register?"

Ione could not answer.

That silence was honest.

Liora looked at the new form again.

`SUBJECT RECOVERY INDEX`.

Recovery index.

This world, having learned to bring people back, had attached a resolution to
death as well. High-resolution death went to trial. Low-resolution death became
an archival error. The recoverable were starting to win the right not to be
owned. The unrecoverable had to borrow even the right to have once existed.

The Last Archive lit up on the counter wall.

No one had called it.

So everyone took a step back.

```text
LOSS AUTHORSHIP REQUEST RECEIVED.
SUBJECT INDEX: INSUFFICIENT.
QUESTION STATUS: MISSTATED.
```

Liora clenched her teeth.

"Not now."

The Archive did not stop.

```text
THE FORM ASKS WHO MAY AUTHOR LOSS.
THE FORM DOES NOT ASK WHO IS ALLOWED TO HAVE BEEN LOST.
```

The third line in the square caught its breath.

The families in the first line heard it too.

The delegates in the second line heard it too.

One of the delegates was already writing it down.

Liora watched the hand. White glove. Thin recording tablet. A quick wrist.
Before the sentence had even cooled out of a person's body, the institution was
preparing to make it a clause.

She opened the case.

The clerk said, quietly:

"Auditor Sen, a new mark without a review process—"

"This isn't a mark."

It was a lie.

But some lies were good ones. For a few seconds, at least.

Liora took out a blank seal. One not yet engraved. It was an old Laur custom.
When a failure had not yet earned a name, the seal took on its weight first and
the letters came later.

Sera Venn held her lunchbox.

Noor stood without sitting in any chair.

Ione tied the knot at her wrist tighter in front of the question she could not
answer.

The clerk could not find the form's close button.

And the delegate was still writing.

Liora pressed the blank seal onto the new form. The seal had no ink, but the
screen read the pressed spot as a mark. Laur equipment was fond of that kind of
superstition.

The wall made the letters first.

```text
UNRECOVERED IS NOT UNREAL
```

Someone wept.

Someone moved to clap and stopped.

The delegate wrote this one down too.

Liora looked at him and said:

"This sentence is not a product."

He bowed, politely.

"Of course."

On his tablet a provisional item had already appeared: `unrecovered claimant
class`.

Noor said, low:

"Too late."

"I know."

"You did it anyway."

"I know."

Sera looked at the screen.

"Does this mean my brother gets filed?"

Liora wanted to answer right away.

That was her worst impulse.

The cruelest moment in a person came when they wanted to say they could help
with a thing they could not help.

"No," Liora said.

Sera's face closed.

Liora went on.

"Not yet. But now the form can't process your brother as if he were no one."

"Is that difference large?"

Liora looked at the square. Wet tents, the badly stamped seal, the child
clutching a bracelet, the white-gloved delegate, the people with no ticket
number, the people with one, the intake counter that would not close.

"Today it's small."

"And tomorrow?"

"Tomorrow someone will try to steal it."

Sera shut the lunchbox. This time the lid did not catch. However many times she
pressed it, one side lifted.

"Then today I'll hold it."

That was the first decent sentence anyone had said.

Liora nodded.

The clerk found the temporary suspension request. This time an item had
appeared.

```text
EMERGENCY HOLD:
UNRECOVERED SUBJECT / LOSS AUTHORSHIP DEFECT
```

He looked at Liora.

"Who approves it?"

The question had come back.

Who.

Liora did not answer.

Instead she closed the case.

The Last Archive added a final line.

```text
SECOND ARC OPENED.
MERCY HAS FOUND ITS MARKET.
```

This time Liora barely even smiled.

Outside, the seal-makers were already memorizing the new sentence.

`UNRECOVERED IS NOT UNREAL`.

A sentence that was hard to pronounce would sell for more.

That, too, Liora knew.
